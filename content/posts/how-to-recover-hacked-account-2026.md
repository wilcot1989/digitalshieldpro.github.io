My phone rang at 11 PM on a Tuesday last year. It was my cousin: "Someone is posting from my Instagram account and I can't log in. What do I do?"

Over the next 90 minutes, I walked her through the recovery process while the attacker was actively using her account. We recovered it. The damage was limited to a few embarrassing posts that we got removed, and she had control back before midnight.

Account compromise happens to careful people. The 2024 breach of a major healthcare provider exposed over 100 million credential pairs. Password reuse is still endemic. Phishing attacks have become sophisticated enough to fool security professionals. When it happens, the first 30 minutes matter enormously.

This guide is the playbook I use when helping people recover compromised accounts. I have organized it by scenario — what to do when you can still log in, what to do when you are locked out, and specific steps for each major platform.

## The First 30 Minutes: What Matters Most

When an account is compromised, you are in a race with the attacker. Their goal is to lock you out — change the recovery email and phone number, disable 2FA, remove trusted devices — so that you cannot reclaim the account. Your goal is to get back in and change those same settings before they do.

Here is the priority order regardless of platform:

1. **Attempt login immediately.** If you can get in, do not waste time — go straight to Security Settings.
2. **Change the password.** Use a long, random password from a password manager. This logs out most sessions including the attacker's.
3. **Check active sessions and log out all devices.** Most platforms have a "Where you're logged in" view. Log out everything.
4. **Verify recovery email and phone number.** Confirm the attacker has not changed these to their own contact information.
5. **Enable 2FA if not already active.** With a compromised password, 2FA is the only thing preventing re-entry.
6. **Check connected apps.** Remove any apps authorized to your account in the last 24 hours that you did not authorize.
7. **Scan for damage.** Check sent messages, posts, account settings changes, and any financial or connected accounts.

Now let me go through platform-specific recovery for the services where I have helped people most.

## Google Account Recovery

Google is particularly important because your Google account often controls Gmail, YouTube, Google Drive, Google Pay, Android device management, and every app you have signed into with "Login with Google."

### If You Can Still Log In

1. Go to myaccount.google.com
2. Click Security in the left sidebar
3. Under "Recent security activity" — review any suspicious sign-ins
4. Click "Manage all devices" under Your Devices — log out unrecognized devices
5. Under How you sign in to Google: change your password
6. Review and confirm recovery email and phone number
7. Under Third-party apps with Google account access — remove anything you did not authorize
8. Enable 2-Step Verification if not active (use authenticator app, not SMS)

Check Gmail specifically: look at Sent folder for emails you did not send. Look at Settings (gear icon) → See all settings → Filters and Blocked Addresses for any forwarding rules the attacker may have set to exfiltrate your email going forward.

### If You Are Locked Out

1. Go to accounts.google.com/signin/recovery
2. Google attempts to verify your identity using the last password you remember, your recovery email, your recovery phone, a trusted device, or backup codes
3. If you have access to your recovery email or phone, use those to receive a verification code
4. If you have no recovery options accessible, Google provides an identity verification form that takes 3-7 business days

Important: the more recovery options you set up before being hacked, the easier recovery is. Set up both a recovery email and a recovery phone number. These are worth doing right now if you have not already.

### After Recovery

- Go to myaccount.google.com/data-and-privacy → Third-party apps and services and review every connected app
- Check Google Pay at pay.google.com for any unauthorized transactions
- If the attacker had access to Gmail for any period, assume they read sensitive emails. Consider what information was exposed and act accordingly (change passwords on banking sites, notify affected parties)

## Facebook Account Recovery

Facebook hacks are common because Facebook accounts are valuable — they can be used for scam ads, impersonation, and accessing Facebook Marketplace for fraud.

### If You Can Still Log In

1. Go to facebook.com/settings/security
2. Under Where You're Logged In — End Activity on any unrecognized sessions
3. Change your password under Login → Change Password
4. Review your email address and phone number under Personal Information
5. Under Security and Login → Two-Factor Authentication — enable if not active
6. Check Recent Emails from Facebook to see if any account changes were made recently
7. Go to facebook.com/settings/apps and remove any recently authorized apps

Check your Timeline and Ads Activity for any posts or ads the attacker may have created.

### If You Are Locked Out

Facebook's account recovery is notoriously difficult. Your options in order of reliability:

**Option 1: Use a linked mobile number.**
Go to facebook.com/login → Forgot password → Enter your email or phone number → SMS recovery code

**Option 2: Use a trusted friend (if you set up Trusted Contacts).**
If you previously set up 3-5 Trusted Contacts, they can each give you a piece of a recovery code. At the recovery screen, select "No longer have access to these?" → "Recover with the help of friends"

**Option 3: Identity verification.**
Go to facebook.com/hacked → Report that someone else is using your account. Facebook's identity verification process can take days to weeks and involves submitting government-issued photo ID.

**If you cannot recover at all:** File a report at facebook.com/hacked anyway to limit the damage the attacker can do with your account. Report the compromised account through Facebook's Help Center for fraudulent accounts.

### After Recovery

- Review and remove any posts or ads created by the attacker (Go to Activity Log)
- Contact people who received suspicious messages from your account
- Check Facebook Pay for unauthorized transactions
- If the attacker changed your email or phone, check whether those changes affected verification on other accounts using the same email

## Instagram Account Recovery

Instagram compromises often originate from phishing — a fake "Instagram Security" email claiming your account will be disabled unless you verify your login.

### If You Can Still Log In

1. Go to your profile → three lines menu → Settings and activity
2. Security → Password → Change password
3. Account → Active sessions → Log out all other sessions
4. Security → Two-factor authentication → Enable (use authenticator app)
5. Account → Apps and websites → Remove unrecognized apps

### If You Are Locked Out

Instagram's recovery process:

**Option 1: Send recovery link to email or phone**
Open Instagram → Forgot password? → Enter email or phone → Check for a reset link or code

**Option 2: Need more help?**
If the attacker changed your recovery email and phone, select "Need more help?" at the bottom of the recovery screen. Instagram will guide you through video selfie verification — you record yourself following on-screen instructions (look left, right, blink) to verify your identity against your profile photos.

**Option 3: Submit a support request**
Instagram's Help Center has a form for "I can't access this email or phone number anymore." This process can take several days and requires photo ID if you used real photos on your account.

**If your account was deleted:** Instagram keeps deleted accounts for 30 days before permanent deletion. Contact Instagram support immediately if your account was deleted by an attacker.

### After Recovery

- Check Stories, Posts, and DMs for anything sent by the attacker
- Notify followers if the attacker posted anything problematic
- Review connected apps and remove unrecognized connections

## Microsoft / Outlook Account Recovery

Microsoft accounts control Outlook email, OneDrive storage, Xbox accounts, Office subscriptions, and any services signed in with Microsoft.

### If You Can Still Log In

1. Go to account.microsoft.com
2. Click Security → Advanced Security Options
3. Review Recent Activity for unrecognized sign-ins
4. Change your password under Change Password
5. Under Sign-in preferences → Remove unrecognized devices
6. Under Two-step verification — enable if not active (use authenticator app)

### If You Are Locked Out

1. Go to account.live.com/acsr (Account Self-Service Recovery)
2. Microsoft asks for your email, alternative email, or phone number
3. If those options are unavailable, click "I don't have any of these" to reach the account recovery form
4. The recovery form asks detailed questions about your account history (email addresses you've sent to, passwords you remember, billing information) — answer as many as possible
5. Recovery typically takes 24-72 hours; Microsoft sends a decision via the alternative email you provide on the form

## Twitter / X Account Recovery

1. Go to x.com/account/begin_password_reset
2. Enter your email or phone number for a reset link
3. If the attacker changed these, click "I don't have access to these" → Submit a support request with account details

Twitter/X support for locked accounts has become less responsive since 2023 changes to the platform. If automated recovery fails, expect longer wait times for manual review.

## What to Do After Any Account Recovery

Recovering the account is step one. These follow-up actions reduce the impact and prevent repeat compromise.

### Step 1: Audit Connected Accounts

Any account that used "Login with [Hacked Service]" needs immediate password reset and security review. If your Google account was compromised, check every service where you used "Login with Google."

### Step 2: Check for Sensitive Data Exposure

For each compromised account, think about what an attacker who had access for [X hours] could have seen:
- Email account: every email, contact list, email-based password resets for other services
- Social account: direct messages, contacts, location history, payment methods
- Cloud storage: all stored files and documents

Act on exposure. If banking credentials were in your email, contact your bank. If personal photos were in cloud storage, consider whether any are sensitive enough to warrant concern.

### Step 3: Run a Full Security Audit

After any account compromise:

1. Check haveibeenpwned.com — enter your email to see if it appears in known data breaches
2. Change passwords on any other accounts where you reused the compromised password
3. Enable 2FA on every important account (email, banking, social media, password manager)
4. Review your password manager for reused passwords — update them all

### Step 4: Prevent Recurrence

The most common sources of account compromise:

**Credential stuffing:** Your email/password combination from a previous breach was tested against other services. Solution: unique passwords everywhere, stored in a password manager.

**Phishing:** You entered your credentials on a fake website. Solution: hardware security keys or passkeys make phishing impossible at the credential level. At minimum, stop clicking email links — navigate to services directly.

**SIM swap:** Attacker transferred your phone number to their SIM and intercepted SMS 2FA codes. Solution: switch from SMS 2FA to authenticator app or hardware key.

**Malware:** A keylogger or credential-stealing malware captured your password. Solution: run a full antivirus scan immediately after account recovery. Consider whether the device should be wiped.

### Step 5: Documentation

If financial damage occurred or you plan to file a police report, document everything:
- Screenshots of suspicious activity with timestamps
- Records of when you discovered the compromise
- What the attacker did while they had access
- Any communications you can identify as coming from the attacker

This documentation is required for fraud claims with banks and creates a record for any law enforcement involvement.

## Preventing the Next Compromise

The best account recovery guide is one you never need to use. Here is what actually prevents compromise.

**Use a password manager.** Every account gets a unique, randomly generated password. This eliminates credential stuffing as an attack vector entirely. If one service is breached, no other account is affected.

**Enable 2FA on every important account.** Priority order: email accounts first (they control password resets for everything else), then banking and financial accounts, then social media, then everything else.

**Use an authenticator app, not SMS.** Authenticator apps generate codes that cannot be intercepted via SIM swap. Google Authenticator, Authy, and the 2FA built into password managers are all good options.

**Keep recovery options current.** Regularly verify that your recovery email and phone number for each important account are still accessible. Recovery options set up years ago may be for old phone numbers or email addresses you no longer control.

**Check haveibeenpwned.com quarterly.** Free breach monitoring tells you when your email appears in a new data breach. When it does, change passwords on affected services immediately.

**Be suspicious of security emails.** Legitimate services never ask for your password. "Your account will be suspended unless you verify" emails are almost always phishing. Navigate directly to the service rather than clicking email links.

The inconvenient truth is that account security in 2026 is still primarily the user's responsibility. Platform security has improved enormously, but credential theft, phishing, and social engineering remain effective because they exploit human behavior rather than technical vulnerabilities. The habits above — unique passwords, strong 2FA, regular monitoring — are the most effective protection against the most common attacks.

## Account Recovery for Less Common but Important Platforms

### Apple ID Recovery

Apple ID controls iCloud storage, App Store purchases, Find My iPhone, and iMessage. Losing access can be severe.

If you can still sign in:
1. appleid.apple.com → Security → Change password
2. Review trusted phone numbers and email addresses
3. Enable two-factor authentication (required for most new Apple services)
4. Check Devices to remove any unrecognized devices

If you are locked out:
- iforgot.apple.com for standard recovery via trusted phone or email
- Apple Recovery Key: if you set up a Recovery Key (a 28-character code), that bypasses all other recovery methods — but losing the key AND being locked out of trusted devices means a formal account recovery process lasting days to weeks
- Contact Apple Support for identity verification-based recovery

### Amazon Account Recovery

Amazon accounts store payment methods, order history, and may include Prime Video, Alexa devices, and AWS credentials.

1. amazon.com → Account & Lists → Forgot your password
2. Recovery via registered email or phone
3. If compromised: after recovery, check payment methods (delete any unrecognized cards), review order history for fraudulent purchases, and check third-party app authorizations

### PayPal Account Recovery

PayPal accounts contain financial information and transaction history. Compromise can result in unauthorized payments.

1. paypal.com/signin → Having trouble logging in?
2. Recover via email or phone
3. After recovery: immediately check transaction history for unauthorized payments and dispute any fraudulent transactions through PayPal's Resolution Center
4. Contact your bank if funds were transferred out

### Banking and Financial Accounts

If your banking app or online banking is compromised:
1. Call the bank's fraud line immediately (number on the back of your debit card)
2. Report unauthorized transactions — consumer protection law requires banks to investigate
3. Request new card numbers if card details were exposed
4. Ask the bank to flag your account for heightened monitoring

## Building a Resilient Account Security System

After going through account recovery once — whether for yourself or helping someone else — the priority should be ensuring it never has to happen again. Here is the system I recommend building.

**Layer 1: Password Manager**
Every account gets a unique, randomly generated 20+ character password stored in a password manager. I use Bitwarden (open-source, audited, excellent free tier). The password manager has a strong master password (passphrase: four random words) and is protected by authenticator-based 2FA.

**Layer 2: Authenticator App 2FA**
Every important account (email, banking, social media, developer tools) gets 2FA enabled using an authenticator app. Google Authenticator, Authy, and Aegis (Android, open-source) are all good choices. Store the TOTP backup codes in your password manager or an encrypted document.

**Layer 3: Verified Recovery Options**
For every account, verify that the recovery email and phone number are current, accessible, and protected themselves. Your recovery email is only useful if it is also secured — it needs its own strong password and 2FA.

**Layer 4: Regular Monitoring**
Check haveibeenpwned.com monthly or set up email alerts (they notify you when your email appears in new breaches). Review active sessions on major platforms every few months. Look at recent login activity in Google and Microsoft account settings.

**Layer 5: Documentation**
Keep a secure document (encrypted, in your password manager or a secured note) that lists recovery options for each important account. Include: backup codes, trusted contacts set up (for Facebook), recovery email addresses, phone numbers used for verification. This document is what saves you when everything else fails.

The difference between account recovery taking 10 minutes versus weeks comes down almost entirely to preparation. Every backup code you save, every recovery email you verify, every 2FA setup you complete before a crisis shortens recovery time dramatically. Build the system when nothing is wrong — it is far easier to prepare than to scramble.
