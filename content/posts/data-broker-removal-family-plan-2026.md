## Why Family Coverage Matters More Than You Think

Data broker profiles do not exist in isolation. The information brokers hold on you is interconnected:

- Your address connects to your spouse's listing (same household)
- Your phone number connects to family members who call you (call records)
- Your relative associations explicitly name your parents, siblings, and children
- Your vehicle registration often lists household members

When I looked up my Spokeo listing at the start of my privacy project, the "Associates" section listed my wife, my brother, and two friends. Anyone who found my listing had an instant pathway to finding information about the people closest to me. That is why removing just one person's data from a household is only half the job.

## Who Needs a Family Plan vs. Individual Plans for Each Person?

**Get a family plan if:**
- Two or more household members want coverage
- You want simplified billing under one account
- The per-person discount saves meaningful money at your household size
- You want to protect a spouse, elderly parent, or adult child

**Get individual plans if:**
- Family members have very different budgets or preferences
- Family members are in different countries (Incogni's GDPR coverage only helps EU residents)
- You want different service tiers for different family members (e.g., premium for yourself, basic for a parent)

**For most households**, a family plan is simpler and more cost-effective. The billing consolidation alone reduces friction, and the per-person discount at two or more people is usually 15–40%.

## Special Case: Protecting Elderly Parents

Elderly adults are disproportionately targeted by scams that use data broker information. Phone scams, grandparent scams, Medicare fraud, and social engineering attacks frequently use publicly available personal data to make the scammer seem credible.

Common tactics I have documented:
- Scammers call elderly targets using their first name and referencing a real relative (whose name they found in the "Associates" section of a data broker listing)
- They reference the target's real address to establish credibility
- They know recent life events (property sale, vehicle registration) from public records that feed brokers

Removing your parents' data from people search sites is one of the most practical steps you can take to reduce their exposure to these attacks. Most family plan structures allow you to add non-household members — meaning you can include your parents under your family plan even if they live elsewhere.

**Practical tip:** When setting up coverage for elderly parents, make sure to include their full address history, as data brokers frequently have listings tied to old addresses (which older adults have more of). Missing an old address means missing the listings tied to it.

## Frequently Overlooked: Protecting Your Address Before Removal

One mistake I see people make when setting up family coverage: they focus entirely on removal and forget about prevention. A few steps that meaningfully reduce how quickly your data re-appears after removal:

**Use a USPS PO Box for non-essential mail.** Every package you receive at your home address is a potential data source. Many data brokers pull from shipping records.

**Opt out of marketing lists proactively.** DMAchoice.org lets you opt out of direct mail marketing, which feeds some data broker databases. This is free and takes about 20 minutes.

**Check your state's voter registration privacy options.** Many states allow you to keep your residential address confidential on voter rolls, which are a major data broker source. California, Washington, and several other states offer "confidential voter" status for residents with safety concerns.

**Review your social media privacy settings.** While social media platforms are not traditional data brokers, the information you make public there gets scraped and incorporated into broker databases.

None of these prevent data collection entirely, but they slow re-listing — which means your removal service needs to send re-submission requests less frequently.
