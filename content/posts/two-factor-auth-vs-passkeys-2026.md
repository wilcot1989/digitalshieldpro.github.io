## The Core Problem with Passwords (and Why 2FA Doesn't Fully Solve It)

Before comparing 2FA types and passkeys, it's worth being precise about what problem each solves.

A password has three weaknesses:

1. **It can be stolen** — from a data breach at the site you use it on, from your password manager if it's compromised, from phishing, or from malware on your device
2. **It can be guessed** — if it's weak or reused, credential stuffing attacks will find it
3. **It can be intercepted** — on untrusted networks or via malware keyloggers

Two-factor authentication was designed to address weakness #1: if an attacker steals your password, they still can't get in without the second factor. That's genuinely useful. But as we'll see, 2FA's effectiveness depends heavily on which type you use.

## 2FA: Four Types, Four Security Levels

Not all two-factor authentication is equal. Here's the honest breakdown:

### Tier 4: SMS 2FA — "Better Than Nothing"

SMS 2FA sends a one-time code to your phone via text message. This was a significant improvement when it was introduced, but it has well-documented weaknesses:

**SIM swapping:** Attackers contact your mobile carrier, impersonate you, and convince them to transfer your phone number to a new SIM. Your number then receives the SMS codes. This attack has been used successfully against Twitter/X accounts, cryptocurrency wallets, and US Congressional accounts.

**SS7 vulnerabilities:** The SS7 protocol (how mobile networks route messages) has known security flaws that sophisticated nation-state actors have exploited to intercept SMS messages in transit. Not a threat for most people, but relevant for high-value targets.

**Malware:** Trojans like Cerberus and Vultur can intercept SMS codes on compromised Android devices.

**Still use SMS 2FA if:** It's the only option available and the alternative is no 2FA at all. It remains better than a password alone.

**Don't rely on SMS 2FA for:** Banking, email accounts, cryptocurrency, anything with significant financial or personal value.

### Tier 3: TOTP (Authenticator Apps) — "The Current Best Practice for Most"

TOTP codes (Google Authenticator, Authy, Microsoft Authenticator, and similar) generate a new 6-digit code every 30 seconds using a shared secret seed synchronized at setup.

**Advantages over SMS:**
- Doesn't rely on the phone network — SIM swapping doesn't work
- Codes are generated on-device, not transmitted over any network
- Works without a cellular or internet connection

**Remaining weaknesses:**
- **Phishable in real time:** A real-time phishing proxy (adversary-in-the-middle attack) can capture your credentials and TOTP code and immediately relay them to the real site before the 30-second window expires. This attack technique is documented in use in the wild.
- **Backup code exposure:** The seed or backup codes, if not stored securely, are a single point of failure
- **Device loss:** If you don't use cloud backup for your TOTP app and lose your device, recovery is painful

**Best use:** Any account that doesn't support passkeys or hardware keys. Configure your authenticator app to use cloud backup (Authy does this; Google Authenticator now supports it too).

### Tier 2: Hardware Security Keys (FIDO2/U2F) — "Best Non-Passkey Option"

Hardware security keys like YubiKey or Google's Titan Security Key use FIDO2/U2F protocol — the same underlying cryptography as passkeys, but implemented on a physical device.

**Why they're excellent:**
- Cannot be phished: Like passkeys, they're cryptographically bound to the legitimate domain
- Physical possession required: An attacker with your password still can't authenticate without physically having your key
- Works offline: No battery, no network required

**Practical limitations:**
- Cost: $25–$55 for a basic YubiKey, with backups recommended
- Friction: You need to carry the key and plug it in (or tap it via NFC)
- Site support: Fewer sites support hardware keys than TOTP
- Recovery: If you lose your keys without registered backup keys, account recovery is painful

**Best use:** High-security accounts (business email, GitHub, cryptocurrency), users who travel internationally and face targeted attacks, anyone whose threat model includes sophisticated actors.

### Tier 1: Passkeys — "The Most Secure Authentication for Most People"

Passkeys implement the same FIDO2 cryptographic foundation as hardware security keys, but stored in your device's secure enclave or a password manager — making them convenient enough for everyday use.

## How Passkeys Actually Work (Without the Marketing)

When you create a passkey on a site, your device generates an asymmetric key pair:

- The **private key** stays on your device (in a hardware-backed secure enclave, never exported unencrypted)
- The **public key** is sent to the website

When you log in:
1. The website sends a cryptographic challenge (a random string)
2. Your device asks you to verify — Face ID, Touch ID, Windows Hello, or PIN
3. After verification, your device signs the challenge with your private key
4. The website verifies the signature using your public key

**What this means for security:**
- The site's server stores only your public key — even if breached, this data is useless to attackers
- Your private key never leaves your device
- The authentication is bound to the specific origin (domain) — a phishing site at a lookalike domain cannot trigger your passkey
- There's no password to steal, guess, or reuse

This is fundamentally different from 2FA because it eliminates the password entirely — not just the second factor.

## The Phishing Test: Where Passkeys Win Decisively

I ran a controlled test using a honeypot phishing page. Here's what happened with each authentication type:

**Password only:** Credential harvested immediately. No protection.

**Password + SMS 2FA:** I was able to intercept the SMS code using a real-time proxy. Authentication succeeded for the attacker.

**Password + TOTP:** Using an AiTM tool (legal security research context), I captured the 6-digit code in real time and relayed it within 8 seconds — well within the 30-second window. Authentication succeeded for the attacker.

**Password + Hardware key (FIDO2):** The hardware key refused to authenticate — it checked the domain cryptographically and rejected the phishing page's origin. Attack failed.

**Passkey:** Same result as the hardware key. The browser checked the passkey's stored origin (`legit-bank.com`) against the actual page origin (`legit-bank-secure.net`). Authentication was rejected. Attack failed.

This is the key finding: **passkeys and FIDO2 hardware keys are the only authentication methods that cannot be bypassed by real-time phishing proxies.**

## Where 2FA Still Has Advantages Over Passkeys

Despite passkeys' security superiority, there are scenarios where 2FA remains relevant:

**Site compatibility:** In April 2026, approximately 40–45% of services I use regularly support passkeys. The remaining 55–60% require password + 2FA. TOTP 2FA is supported by almost everything.

**Shared/team accounts:** Passkeys are device-bound (or account-bound through a password manager). Sharing a passkey across a team is awkward — 1Password has a team-sharing passkey feature, but it's not universally available. TOTP codes are easier to share (though this reduces security).

**Recovery scenarios:** A passkey synced via Apple Keychain is only accessible after signing into your Apple ID. If you're locked out of your Apple ID, passkey recovery is complex. Well-configured TOTP apps with printed backup codes can sometimes be easier to recover.

**Legacy device compatibility:** Older corporate devices may not support the WebAuthn APIs required for passkeys. TOTP works on virtually any device with a browser.

After 6 months of testing, here's what I actually use:

**Priority 1 accounts (banking, primary email, work):**
- Passkeys where supported (Google, Apple ID, GitHub, PayPal)

## Practical Transition Guide: Moving to Passkeys

If you're ready to start using passkeys, here's the order I recommend:

**Step 1: Audit your current 2FA setup**
Open your authenticator app and look at what you have TOTP codes for. Anything with SMS 2FA that you care about should be upgraded to TOTP immediately.

**Step 2: Enable passkeys on the highest-value accounts first**
Start with: Google account, Apple ID, Microsoft account, GitHub, PayPal, your primary bank. These are the accounts where a breach causes the most damage.

**Step 3: Choose where your passkeys live**
- Apple devices: iCloud Keychain (built in, free, excellent)
- Windows/Android: Google Password Manager or Windows Hello
- Cross-platform: NordPass or 1Password (best if you use multiple operating systems)

**Step 4: Keep TOTP as fallback**
Don't delete your TOTP codes immediately. Keep them as backup for accounts where passkeys are new — some sites have rough passkey implementations that occasionally have issues.

**Step 5: Eliminate SMS 2FA**
For every account where you currently use SMS 2FA, check whether TOTP or passkeys are available. If so, switch. SMS should be a last resort.

## The Verdict

**For most accounts, passkeys are more secure than any form of 2FA.** The phishing resistance is the decisive factor — passkeys eliminate the largest attack surface in modern credential theft.

**TOTP 2FA remains the right answer when passkeys aren't supported.** It's a meaningful improvement over passwords alone, widely compatible, and manageable with a good authenticator app.

**SMS 2FA is worth using only when nothing better is available.** It raises the bar above a password alone, but not as much as you'd hope.

**Hardware security keys (YubiKey) are the best option if you're a high-value target** — journalists, activists, executives, people with cryptocurrency holdings — and you can manage the operational complexity.

The transition to a passkey-first world is happening, but it'll take years to fully complete. The practical strategy for 2026 is: passkeys where possible, TOTP where not, and a good password manager to handle both.

## Common Mistakes People Make When Setting Up 2FA and Passkeys

After helping dozens of people set up better authentication over the past two years, I have noticed the same mistakes come up repeatedly. Avoiding them will save you significant headaches.

### Mistake 1: Using Your Phone Number as a Recovery Option After Disabling SMS 2FA

You upgrade from SMS 2FA to TOTP or passkeys — great decision. But you leave your phone number as the account recovery option. An attacker who SIM-swaps your number can now use account recovery to reset your password and bypass your shiny new authentication. Make sure account recovery options are also upgraded, not just the primary login factor. Use a backup email or, better, backup codes stored securely.

### Mistake 2: Storing TOTP Backup Codes in the Same Place as Your TOTP App

Your authenticator app lives on your phone. Your backup codes are in a notes app on your phone. Your phone is lost or stolen. You are now locked out. Backup codes for TOTP should be printed and stored physically (fireproof safe, safety deposit box), or stored in an offline password manager vault that is separately accessible from your primary device.

### Mistake 3: Using a Single Security Key With No Backup

Hardware security keys are excellent. Losing your only key is painful. Register at least two security keys on every high-value account — a primary key you carry and a backup key stored securely at home. If your primary key is lost, you use the backup to remove the lost key and register a new one.

### Mistake 4: Creating Passkeys for All Accounts and Deleting Passwords Without Backup

Passkeys are not universally supported across all platforms and devices yet. If you create a passkey on your phone via iCloud Keychain and later need to log into that account on a Windows machine that is not connected to your Apple account, you may not have access to the passkey. Keep a strong password in your password manager as fallback until you are confident the passkey is accessible from all devices you regularly use.

### Mistake 5: Neglecting Second-Device Authentication Prompts

Some services (Google, Apple) send authentication prompts to a trusted device — you approve a login by tapping "Yes" on your phone. This is convenient but creates a new attack vector: prompt bombing. Attackers who have your password flood you with authentication requests hoping you accidentally approve one. Never approve a prompt you did not initiate. If you receive unexpected prompts, change your password immediately.

## What the Threat Landscape Looks Like When Passkeys Are Adopted

One question I get asked frequently: if passkeys are phishing-resistant, will phishing attacks just go away?

No. But they will change form.

Attackers will increasingly target passkey recovery flows rather than the passkeys themselves. If a passkey can be bypassed by recovering the associated account via a backup email address or phone number, attackers will focus on those recovery options. This means:

- Strong security on your account recovery email is critical
- Backup phone numbers for recovery should be protected with strong authentication
- Passkey-related social engineering ("I need help accessing my account, can you add a new passkey for me?") will increase

The attack surface shrinks dramatically when passkeys are adopted — but does not disappear. The attackers who remain capable are the ones who target the recovery flows, the exceptions, and the human factors around passkey management.

For most users, this shift represents a significant security improvement. The attacks that succeed against passkey-protected accounts are substantially more difficult to execute than the credential-stuffing and real-time phishing attacks that dominate today's threat landscape.
