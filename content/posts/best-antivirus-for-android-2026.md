## Do You Even Need Antivirus on Android?

This is the most common question we hear, so let us address it head-on before diving into the rankings.

The short answer is **yes, you do**. Here is why:

**Android's open ecosystem is a double-edged sword.** The ability to sideload apps, use third-party app stores, and customize your device is one of Android's greatest strengths. It is also its biggest security weakness. Unlike iOS, which restricts app installation almost entirely to the App Store, Android gives users the freedom to install software from virtually anywhere. That freedom means malicious apps can reach your device through APK downloads, unofficial app stores, links in text messages, and even the occasional slip through Google Play's vetting process.

**Google Play is not malware-free.** Despite Google's efforts, malicious apps regularly make it onto the Play Store. In 2025 alone, researchers identified over **200 malicious apps on Google Play** that collectively accumulated more than 8 million downloads before being removed. These ranged from adware disguised as utility apps to sophisticated banking trojans masquerading as PDF readers.

**Phishing has gone mobile.** SMS phishing (smishing) increased by **45% in 2025**, with attackers sending convincing messages that impersonate banks, delivery services, and government agencies. These messages contain links to credential-harvesting sites that look identical to legitimate login pages. Without web protection from a security app, your browser will not warn you.

**Public Wi-Fi remains dangerous.** Android devices automatically connect to known Wi-Fi networks, and attackers exploit this by setting up rogue access points with common names like "Starbucks_WiFi" or "Airport_Free." A security app with network scanning can detect these threats before your data is compromised.

If you use your phone for banking, email, work, or storing personal information of any kind, antivirus protection is not optional. It is essential.

## What to Look for in an Android Antivirus App

Choosing the right security app goes beyond malware detection rates. Here are the key features and considerations that matter most on Android.

### Real-time scanning

This is non-negotiable. Your antivirus must scan apps, files, and downloads in real-time as they are accessed or installed. On-demand scanning alone is not enough because it only catches threats when you manually run a scan, by which time a malicious app could have already exfiltrated your data. All five products in our ranking provide robust real-time protection.

### App permission auditing

Android apps routinely request permissions they do not need. A weather app requesting access to your contacts and SMS messages is a red flag, but most users do not review permissions closely enough to catch it. A good security app continuously monitors app permissions and flags any that are excessive or suspicious. Bitdefender and Kaspersky excel in this area.

### Anti-theft features

Phones get lost and stolen. Anti-theft features allow you to remotely locate, lock, and wipe your device, protecting your personal data even if the physical device is gone. Some apps, like Kaspersky, include advanced features like SIM Watch and front-camera snapshots of the thief.

### Wi-Fi network scanning

Public Wi-Fi is one of the most common attack vectors for mobile devices. Your security app should scan networks for vulnerabilities and alert you before you connect to a compromised access point. If you regularly connect to public networks, pairing your antivirus with a reliable VPN is a smart move. See our guide on [how to set up a VPN](/posts/how-to-set-up-vpn-2026/) for step-by-step instructions.

### SMS phishing (smishing) protection

With SMS phishing attacks increasing sharply, the ability to scan incoming text messages for malicious links is increasingly important. Bitdefender and Kaspersky both offer this feature, while Norton, ESET, and Surfshark currently do not.

### Battery and performance impact

A security app that drains your battery or makes your phone sluggish is counterproductive. In my testing, Bitdefender and ESET had the lowest resource footprint, while Norton had the highest. On modern flagship phones, even Norton's impact was acceptable, but on older or budget devices, choosing a lightweight option matters.

## Android Security Best Practices Beyond Antivirus

Even with the best antivirus app installed, your behavior matters. Here are the practices that security experts recommend for Android users in 2026.

### Keep your phone updated

Android security patches are released monthly. Install them as soon as they are available. These patches close vulnerabilities that malware actively exploits. If your phone manufacturer no longer provides updates, seriously consider upgrading to a device that does.

### Stick to the Google Play Store

While the Play Store is not perfect, it is dramatically safer than third-party app stores and direct APK downloads. Google removed over **2.3 million apps** from the Play Store in 2025 for policy violations, and its automated scanning catches most malicious submissions before they reach users.

### Review app permissions

Before installing any app, check what permissions it requests. After installation, periodically review permissions in Settings > Privacy > Permission Manager. Revoke any permissions that seem excessive for the app's intended function.

### Use strong, unique passwords

Pair your antivirus with a reliable password manager to ensure every account has a unique, strong password. Our [Best Password Managers 2026](/posts/best-password-managers-2026/) guide covers the top options, and our article on [how to create strong passwords](/posts/how-to-create-strong-passwords-2026/) explains what makes a password truly secure.

### Enable two-factor authentication

Enable 2FA on every account that supports it, especially email, banking, and social media. Use an authenticator app rather than SMS-based codes, since SIM swapping attacks can intercept text messages.

### Be skeptical of links

Whether they arrive via text message, email, social media, or messaging app, treat unexpected links with suspicion. Do not tap links in messages from unknown senders, and verify unexpected messages from known contacts by reaching out through a different channel.
