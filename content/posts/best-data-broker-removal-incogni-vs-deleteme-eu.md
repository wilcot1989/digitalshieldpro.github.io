## Why Data Broker Removal Matters More in 2026

Three trends have converged to make data broker exposure riskier than five years ago:

**1. AI-powered social engineering:** attackers now use LLMs to generate highly personalized phishing emails using data sourced from brokers. Your email, name, employer, home address, relatives — all available. Generic phishing filters are less effective when the email reads as if the sender knows you.

**2. Data aggregation at scale:** brokers now cross-reference dozens of sources. A profile that was 10 data points in 2020 is now 40–60 data points in 2026. Combined, this creates a detailed picture of your life patterns.

**3. Harassment and doxxing:** the barrier to doxxing has dropped. Anyone with €20 can purchase a people-search report with your address, relatives, and daily patterns. This is a direct physical safety issue for public figures, domestic violence survivors, and journalists.

## The GDPR Advantage for EU Residents

If you are in the EU or EEA, you have rights that US residents do not:

**Article 15 (Right of access):** you can demand to know what data a broker holds about you.  
**Article 17 (Right to erasure):** you can demand deletion of your data. Brokers must comply unless they have a legal basis to retain it.  
**Article 77 (Right to lodge a complaint):** if a broker ignores your request, you can complain to your national data protection authority (DPA). In the Netherlands that is the Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens.

The key: brokers who operate in the EU or process EU residents' data are subject to GDPR regardless of where the broker is headquartered. A US broker that holds Dutch residents' data is subject to GDPR requests.

[Incogni](/go/incogni) is operated by Surfshark Ltd and launched in 2022. It is currently the leading automated data removal service in the EU market.

The GDPR framing matters. Incogni explicitly sends Article 17 requests for EU users. This carries more legal weight than a simple opt-out request.

Incogni re-submits requests. Data repopulates — brokers re-acquire your data from new sources. Incogni sends removal requests on a recurring basis (approximately every 3 months) as long as your subscription is active.

Price: €6.49/month billed annually. That is €77.88/year. For the time saved on manual requests, I consider this good value.

**What I do not like:**

You cannot see which specific data Incogni found at each broker. The dashboard tells you a broker was contacted and whether they confirmed removal, but not what data they held. For verification purposes, this is frustrating.

Not all 180 brokers are EU-relevant. Some are US-only (Spokeo, BeenVerified). For a Dutch user, maybe 100–120 brokers are genuinely relevant.

Most data broker guides are written by US authors covering US-focused brokers. As a Netherlands-based analyst, I want to address the EU-specific broker landscape.

**EU people-search sites (active as of 2026):**

- Telefoongids.nl — phone lookups and business searches

**The KvK (Chamber of Commerce) caveat:**
Dutch business registrations are public record. If you are a ZZP (freelancer) registered with the KvK, your name and business address are publicly accessible regardless of data broker removal services. Incogni and DeleteMe cannot remove this data — it is legally required public information. Consider using a registered office address (registered mail address via a business center) if you do not want your home address in the KvK register.

**GDPR enforcement on brokers:**
The Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens (AP) has issued fines for GDPR violations by data brokers in 2024–2025, including a €2.3M fine against a Dutch marketing data company for insufficient transparency about data sources. This enforcement activity makes GDPR requests more potent than they were two years ago.

The Incogni dashboard shows four categories for each broker:

## How Data Brokers Actually Get Your Data

Understanding the collection pipeline helps explain why removal is ongoing rather than a one-time fix.

**Primary sources data brokers use:**

**Public records:** property registrations, voter rolls, court records, business filings, birth and marriage records. In the Netherlands, the Basisregistratie Personen (BRP) is a key source. Some brokers have automated access to municipal record feeds.

**Social media:** public profiles on Facebook, LinkedIn, Instagram, Twitter/X. Even "friends only" data can leak through friends who have public profiles or use connected apps.

**Purchase history:** loyalty programs, store cards, and online purchases. When you give your email to a retailer, they may sell anonymized or pseudonymized profiles that include purchase behavior.

**Location data:** apps on your phone that sell location history to data brokers. This includes common apps with location permissions — weather apps, navigation apps, and local service apps are frequent offenders.

**Data broker-to-broker sales:** once your data is in one broker's database, other brokers purchase it. This is why you cannot remove yourself from one database and consider the job done.

**Re-collection timeline:** after removal, most brokers re-acquire your data within 3–6 months from new record scrapes and third-party purchases. This is why ongoing subscription services matter.

## The Legal Basis Framework Under GDPR

When Incogni sends a GDPR Article 17 request, the broker can refuse if they have a legitimate legal basis for retention. The main legal bases:

**Legitimate interests (Art. 6(1)(f)):** brokers often claim that profiling individuals for marketing purposes is a legitimate interest. GDPR requires this to be balanced against the individual's rights. For most people, data removal requests override claimed legitimate interests.

**Public interest:** applies to news archives, public records, and similar uses. A broker claiming public interest for a commercial profiling database is unlikely to succeed before a DPA.

**Legal obligation:** brokers subject to specific regulatory requirements (financial, health data) may retain data for compliance. This rarely applies to general-purpose data brokers.

**What happens when a broker refuses:** Incogni escalates and can flag the refusal for the relevant DPA. DeleteMe offers a similar escalation path. In practice, most commercially motivated brokers comply rather than risk regulatory attention.
