## The Cybersecurity Guide to Home Security Cameras

Here is the part most camera reviews skip: **your security camera can itself become a security vulnerability.** Compromised cameras have been used for everything from spying on families to launching DDoS attacks. Take these steps to keep your cameras secure.

### 1. Change Default Passwords Immediately

Every camera ships with a default password. Change it before the camera even connects to your WiFi. Use your [password manager](/posts/best-password-managers-2026/) to generate a strong, unique password for each camera and its associated app account.

### 2. Enable Two-Factor Authentication

If the camera brand offers 2FA for the app — and most do now — enable it. This prevents attackers from accessing your camera feed even if they steal your password.

### 3. Keep Firmware Updated

Camera manufacturers regularly release security patches. Enable automatic updates if available, or check monthly for firmware updates. Cameras with known unpatched vulnerabilities are prime targets.

### 4. Isolate Cameras on a Separate Network

Create a separate WiFi network (most modern routers support this) for your cameras and other IoT devices. This way, if a camera is compromised, the attacker cannot reach your computers, phones, or NAS where sensitive data lives. Our [home network security guide](/posts/how-to-secure-your-home-network-2026/) walks through this step by step.

This prevents man-in-the-middle attacks on public WiFi that could intercept your camera feeds.

### 6. Audit Camera Permissions

Review what data your camera app collects and what permissions it has on your phone. Disable location tracking, microphone access (unless you use two-way audio), and contact access if not needed.

### 7. Consider Local-Only Storage

Cloud-stored footage is only as secure as the cloud provider. If privacy is a priority, choose cameras with local storage options (Reolink, Eufy) and disable cloud uploads entirely.

## Where to Place Your Cameras

Camera placement matters more than camera quality. A $300 camera aimed at the wrong angle is less useful than a $50 camera aimed at the right one.

### Priority Locations (In Order)

1. **Front door** — 34% of burglars enter through the front door. This is your most important camera position.
2. **Back door** — The second most common entry point, especially for break-ins during daytime.
3. **Driveway/garage** — Monitors vehicles and catches approaching visitors before they reach the door.
4. **First-floor windows** — Especially any not visible from the street.
5. **Side gates** — Often overlooked but a common entry path.

### Placement Tips
- Mount cameras **7-9 feet high**, angled slightly downward
- Avoid pointing cameras directly at bright lights or the setting sun
- Ensure the camera's field of view covers **entry and exit paths**, not just doors
- Use **activity zones** to ignore irrelevant motion (sidewalks, trees)
- Test night vision quality during setup — repositioning may be needed
