## Who Actually Needs a VPN in 2026? (And Who Doesn't)

Before recommending a specific VPN, I want to address the honest question of whether you actually need one. Not everyone does — and overselling VPNs is a real problem in the security space.

**Situations where a VPN provides meaningful protection:**

- Using public WiFi regularly (airports, coffee shops, hotels): your traffic is encrypted and cannot be intercepted by someone on the same network
- ISP privacy: your internet provider can see every site you visit. A VPN prevents this. In the EU, ISPs are required to store metadata, making this especially relevant
- Bypassing geographic restrictions: accessing streaming libraries from other countries, accessing sites that may be blocked in certain regions
- Preventing IP-based tracking: advertisers and sites track your IP address as an identifier. A VPN masks your real IP
- Protecting sensitive professional communications: journalists, lawyers, activists, and healthcare workers have specific threat models where VPN use is appropriate

**Situations where a VPN provides less protection than you think:**

- HTTPS is already standard: most websites use HTTPS, meaning traffic is encrypted between your browser and the website regardless of VPN use. A VPN adds another layer, but you were not unprotected before
- Logged into Google/Facebook: these services track you through account login, cookies, and device fingerprinting. A VPN masks your IP but does not prevent account-based tracking
- Malware on your device: a VPN cannot protect against malware already on your machine

**Verdict:** if you use public WiFi more than once a month, care about your ISP not seeing your browsing history, or regularly access geo-restricted content — a VPN is worth the €3/month.

## Setting Up Your VPN: Best Practices for Maximum Privacy

A VPN misconfigured gives false security. Here is how I set up both for maximum protection.

**Step 1: Choose the right protocol**
Always use WireGuard (NordVPN labels it NordLynx). OpenVPN UDP is the fallback for restricted networks. Never use PPTP — it is cryptographically broken.

**Step 2: Enable the kill switch before connecting to any network**
The kill switch prevents DNS leaks when the VPN connection drops. On NordVPN: Settings → Kill Switch → enable. On Surfshark: Settings → VPN Settings → Kill Switch → enable.

**Step 3: Configure DNS settings**
Both use their own DNS servers by default. For extra redundancy, verify that DNS queries route through the VPN tunnel by running a DNS leak test at dnsleaktest.com while connected.

**Step 4: Split tunneling (optional but useful)**
Split tunneling lets specific apps bypass the VPN. Useful for local network access (NAS, smart home devices) or apps that do not work through a VPN.

**NordVPN split tunneling:** Settings → Split Tunneling. Available on Windows and Android. Not on iOS.  
**Surfshark Bypasser:** Settings → VPN Settings → Bypasser. Available on Windows, Android, and macOS.

**Step 5: Check for leaks**
Once configured, visit ipleak.net and browserleaks.com while connected. Verify: your real IP does not appear, DNS servers show the VPN's servers, and WebRTC is not leaking your local IP.

## VPN Protocols: WireGuard, NordLynx, and OpenVPN

Both VPNs offer multiple protocols. Here is what each means in practice.

**WireGuard:** the newest and fastest protocol. Used by default on both NordVPN and Surfshark. Built with 4,000 lines of code versus OpenVPN's 600,000 — leaner, faster, easier to audit. Uses modern cryptography (ChaCha20, Curve25519).

**NordLynx (NordVPN-specific):** NordVPN built a double-NAT system around WireGuard to solve its privacy limitation. Pure WireGuard requires storing your IP on the server to maintain the tunnel — NordLynx eliminates this. This is a meaningful privacy improvement over vanilla WireGuard.

**IKEv2/IPSec:** good on mobile, reconnects faster after switching networks (useful on trains, when switching between WiFi and cellular). NordVPN supports this; Surfshark does too.

**OpenVPN:** the old standard. Slower but battle-tested over 20 years. Use it when WireGuard is blocked.

**Shadowsocks / obfuscated:** for censored networks (China, UAE). NordVPN's obfuscated servers have been more reliable historically.
