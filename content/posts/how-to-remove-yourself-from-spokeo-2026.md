## 1. How to Remove Yourself from Spokeo

Spokeo is one of the largest people search sites in the US, and it is usually one of the first places I find personal information about myself or others. The opt-out process is functional once you know it — but it is deliberately not prominent on the site.

**Time required:** 10 minutes + email confirmation

**Step 1: Find your Spokeo listing**

Go to spokeo.com and search your name. If you have a common name, add your city or state to narrow results. Click through to your profile page and copy the URL from your browser address bar — you will need it in a moment.

**Step 2: Navigate to the opt-out page**

Go to spokeo.com/optout (note: this page is not linked from Spokeo's main navigation). The opt-out URL is: **spokeo.com/optout**

**Step 3: Paste your profile URL**

Paste the URL of your Spokeo listing into the opt-out form. Enter your email address. Complete the CAPTCHA and click "Send Verification Email."

**Step 4: Confirm via email**

Check your email inbox for a confirmation message from Spokeo. Click the confirmation link. Spokeo typically removes your listing within 24–48 hours.

**Step 5: Verify the removal**

After 48 hours, search for yourself on Spokeo again. If the listing still appears, the removal may not have processed correctly. Repeat the opt-out process.

**Important:** If you have multiple listings on Spokeo (they often create separate records for each address), you need to submit a separate opt-out for each listing.

**When will the data come back?** In my experience, Spokeo re-lists data within 3–6 months. Schedule a calendar reminder to re-check.

## 2. How to Remove Yourself from Whitepages

Whitepages is another major aggregator that is often the top result when someone Googles your name.

**Time required:** 15 minutes + phone verification

**Step 1:** Search whitepages.com for your listing and click through to your profile page.

**Step 2:** Scroll to the bottom of your profile page and click "Control your data" or go directly to whitepages.com/suppression_requests

**Step 3:** Click "Submit a suppression request."

**Step 4:** Enter your name and your Whitepages profile URL.

**Step 5:** Whitepages will ask you to verify via phone call or text message. This is their friction mechanism — enter your phone number to receive a verification code.

**Step 6:** Enter the code and submit. Whitepages typically processes removals within a few days.

**Note:** Whitepages is particularly aggressive about re-listing. In my testing, data re-appeared within 2–4 months on Whitepages more consistently than most other brokers.

## 3. How to Remove Yourself from BeenVerified

BeenVerified is one of the more user-friendly opt-out processes among major brokers.

**Time required:** 10 minutes + email confirmation

**Step 1:** Go to beenverified.com/opt-out/search

**Step 2:** Search for your name and select your listing from the results.

**Step 3:** Click "Opt Out" on your profile page.

**Step 4:** Enter your email address and complete the CAPTCHA.

**Step 5:** Click the confirmation link in the email you receive. BeenVerified processes removals within a few days.

## 4. How to Remove Yourself from Intelius

Intelius has a shared opt-out page with PeopleLookup and several other people search sites, as they are owned by the same parent company.

**Time required:** 15 minutes

**Step 1:** Go to intelius.com/opt-out

**Step 2:** Enter your first name, last name, city, and state, then click "Search."

**Step 3:** Locate your listing in the results and click "This is me."

**Step 4:** Provide your email address to confirm. Click the confirmation link.

**Step 5:** Intelius typically processes the removal within 72 hours. Note: submitting once to Intelius also removes you from their affiliated sites including PeopleLookup, USSearch, and iSearch.

## 5. How to Remove Yourself from MyLife

MyLife is the most hostile opt-out experience among major people search sites. They have built their opt-out process to be as friction-heavy as possible.

**Time required:** 30–60 minutes

**Step 1:** Go to mylife.com/privacy/remove-my-information.html

**Step 2:** Search for your profile. When you find it, click "Claim this profile" — yes, you have to create an account to opt out.

**Step 3:** Create a free MyLife account. They will ask for your name, email, and phone number.

**Step 4:** Once logged in, go to your profile settings and find the privacy/visibility options. Set your profile to private or submit a deletion request.

**Alternatively:** Email privacy@mylife.com with your name, address, and a request for deletion under applicable privacy law. This is slower but does not require creating an account.

**Note:** MyLife is notorious for re-listing data quickly and for making the opt-out process as difficult as possible. It is one of the brokers I most recommend automating via a removal service.

**Step 1:** Go to acxiom.com/about-acxiom/privacy/us-consumer-opt-out-form/

## Why Data Keeps Reappearing After You Opt Out

I need to spend some time on this because it is the thing that most frustrates people who do manual opt-outs for the first time. You spend an afternoon removing yourself from 11 sites. You check back three months later and you are listed again. What happened?

Data brokers operate a continuous data acquisition pipeline. They do not just build a database once and sell access to it — they continuously acquire new and updated data from multiple sources. The main sources that cause re-listing after successful opt-out are:

**Public records updates.** Every time you interact with government systems — renewing a vehicle registration, updating your voter registration, filing a court document, recording a property transaction — that information flows back into public records databases that data brokers monitor. Your opt-out suppresses your current listing; it cannot suppress future acquisitions from these new records.

**Inter-broker data purchases.** Data brokers buy data from other data brokers. When you opt out of Spokeo, Spokeo suppresses your record. But Spokeo continues to purchase data updates from LexisNexis, Acxiom, and other data suppliers. When those suppliers send an update that includes your address (derived from their own public records aggregation), Spokeo can re-list you.

**Marketing and retail data feeds.** Credit card companies, retailers, loyalty programs, and subscription services sell or share customer data that ends up in marketing databases. These databases are updated continuously, and the updates can trigger re-listing at brokers you have already opted out of.

**Telephone and directory data.** Caller ID databases and directory services update continuously from carrier data. A new call made from your phone number can cause your number to appear in broker databases that had previously removed it.

This is why manual opt-out is not a one-time activity. It is an indefinite maintenance task. Schedule calendar reminders for 90-day re-checks on the most important sites (Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified, MyLife). Consider whether the maintenance burden is worth it compared to automating the process.

## Special Considerations for High-Risk Individuals

Most people opt out of data broker sites as a general privacy hygiene measure. But some individuals have heightened urgency:

**Domestic abuse survivors:** If you have left an abusive relationship, your data broker listings are a direct safety risk. Some states have address confidentiality programs (ACPs) that provide substitute addresses for victim survivors in state records — reducing the flow of your address into public records. California, Oregon, Washington, and most other states offer ACPs. Apply through your state's Secretary of State office.

**Stalking victims:** If you are being stalked, prioritize removal from people search sites immediately and consider engaging a removal service for comprehensive coverage rather than the manual approach. Document all your listings with timestamps before removal — this documentation can be useful if you need to file for a restraining order.

**Public figures and executives:** People who appear in news articles and public records more than average tend to have more extensive data broker profiles. If you are a CEO, politician, journalist, or public figure of any kind, consider that your data broker exposure is larger than average and that targeted searches specifically for you are more likely.

**Healthcare workers and law enforcement:** Both groups face elevated risk from data broker exposure. Some states have specific protections — California AB 1184 (effective 2022) requires data brokers to honor opt-out requests from peace officers within 10 business days, faster than the standard timeline.

## Verifying Your Removals: How to Check That It Actually Worked

After completing opt-outs, you should verify that your listings have actually been removed. This is an important step that most guides skip.

**Wait the right amount of time.** Do not check immediately after submitting an opt-out. Most brokers need 24–72 hours to process removals. For some brokers, the timeline is longer — MyLife can take up to two weeks.

**Use an incognito browser.** When checking whether your data is still visible, always use incognito or private browsing mode. Your regular browser may have cached the page with your old listing, or your browsing history may affect what search results you see.

**Search multiple ways.** Do not just type your name into the site's search box. Also try:
- Your full name + city
- Your phone number
- Your email address
- Your old address

Some brokers maintain multiple records indexed by different data points. You may have removed the listing under your name but still have a listing under your old phone number that links back to you.

**Check associated profiles.** Even after your main profile is removed, check whether you appear in other people's "Associated Persons" sections. If your spouse's Spokeo profile lists you as an associated person, your name and partial information is still accessible even without a direct listing under your name.

**Document before and after.** Screenshot your listings before submitting opt-outs, and screenshot the "not found" result after successful removal. This documentation is useful if the broker's data reappears and you need to demonstrate a pattern of re-listing.

## After You Opt Out: What To Do Next

**Check Google's Results About You:** Google now has a tool (google.com/search/about-me/) that lets you request removal of personal contact information from Google search results. This is separate from removing your data from broker sites but important for controlling what appears when someone Googles you.

**Opt out of data sharing at the source:** Several companies you interact with sell data that ends up at brokers. Check privacy settings for your financial accounts, mobile carriers, and retail loyalty programs. Many have opt-out options buried in their privacy settings.

**Use a PO Box or mail forwarding service for forms and deliveries.** This prevents your home address from appearing on new public records as frequently.

**Register to vote using a P.O. Box if your state allows it.** Voter registration data is a major source for data brokers, and several states make this data publicly available.

**Request your consumer reports.** Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act, you are entitled to free annual reports from major data brokers that produce consumer reports for background checks: AnnualCreditReport.com covers the credit bureaus. You can also request reports from Acxiom (personalizedmarketing.acxiom.com) and similar companies.
