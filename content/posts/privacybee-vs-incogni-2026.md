## The 8-Week Test: Week-By-Week Breakdown

Before I activated either service, I spent an afternoon documenting my digital footprint. I found myself listed on 47 sites — ranging from major aggregators like Whitepages and Spokeo to smaller regional sites I had never heard of. I noted which sites had which information: home address on 31, phone number on 28, email address on 19, relative names on 22, employer on 9.

This gave me a baseline to measure both services against.

### Weeks 1–2: Onboarding and First Requests

**Incogni** was live within about 10 minutes of signup. The onboarding asked for my name, address, and email, generated a power of attorney-style authorization for me to sign digitally, and immediately began queuing requests. By the end of day one, the dashboard showed 43 requests sent with statuses ranging from "In progress" to "Awaiting response."

**PrivacyBee** took longer to set up. Their onboarding collected more information — including previous addresses and phone numbers — and ran what they call a "privacy risk assessment" before beginning removals. By day three, they had begun sending requests and showed me an initial risk score of 72/100 (higher is worse).

At the two-week mark:
- Incogni: 89 requests sent, 12 confirmed removals, 41 in progress
- PrivacyBee: 61 requests sent, 8 confirmed removals, 31 in progress

Incogni was moving faster out of the gate. PrivacyBee's interface felt more thorough but slower.

### Weeks 3–4: First Confirmations Roll In

By week four, I was seeing meaningful progress from both services. Incogni's dashboard became genuinely useful here — it broke down requests by category (people search sites, marketing databases, risk mitigation brokers, financial data aggregators) which helped me understand where my data was concentrated.

At four weeks:
- Incogni: 134 requests sent, 38 confirmed removals, 61 in progress, 35 unresponsive
- PrivacyBee: 97 requests sent, 31 confirmed removals, 44 in progress, 22 unresponsive

Both services were showing similar confirmation rates (~28–32%) at this point. The difference was volume — Incogni had contacted more brokers.

I also noticed something important: several brokers that PrivacyBee flagged as "confirmed removal" still showed my data when I checked manually. When I raised this with PrivacyBee support, they explained that confirmation means the broker acknowledged the request, not necessarily that the data was deleted yet. This is a meaningful distinction.

### Weeks 5–6: The Reappearance Problem

This was the most educational phase of the test. At week five, I noticed my listing reappeared on Whitepages — a site both services had previously confirmed as removed. I checked Spokeo: same story. My home address was back.

This is the core reality of data broker removal: it is never finished. Brokers continuously pull in new data from public records updates, credit bureau files, and data resellers. A successful removal today does not mean your data stays gone — it means you are in a cycle of removal and re-listing that requires ongoing intervention.

Both services handled re-submissions automatically, which is the main value proposition. Incogni's dashboard clearly flagged the re-listed sites and showed new requests being sent. PrivacyBee's interface was less transparent about this process, though support confirmed it was happening.

## Deep Dive: Incogni's Technical Approach

One thing I wanted to understand better during this test was how each service actually handles the legal mechanics of removal requests. I spent time reading through Incogni's documentation and talking to support.

Incogni submits opt-out and deletion requests using two primary legal frameworks:

**CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act):** Even if you are not a California resident, Incogni submits requests citing CCPA on your behalf. Many brokers accept these requests from non-California residents because verifying residency is more expensive than just processing the request. For California residents, CCPA requests carry legal teeth — brokers must comply within 45 days or face enforcement by the California Privacy Protection Agency.

**GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation):** For European users, Incogni submits Article 17 "right to erasure" requests under GDPR. These are legally binding and must be processed within 30 days. Non-compliance can result in fines up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue — a meaningful enforcement threat that US-only requests do not carry.

Incogni tracks which legal basis it used for each request and shows this in your dashboard. This matters when you are evaluating whether a broker's compliance is legally mandatory or merely voluntary.

PrivacyBee's legal approach is less transparent in their dashboard, though support confirmed they use similar CCPA and GDPR mechanisms where applicable.

The cases where automation worked equally well:

## What Neither Service Will Do

It is important to understand the limits of both services before you sign up:

**They cannot remove you from search engine results.** Google shows data from data broker sites, but removing your listing from Spokeo does not automatically update Google's index. That takes additional steps (Google's Results About You tool, for example).

**They cannot remove data from social media.** Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, and Twitter/X are not data brokers in the traditional sense, and their opt-out process is separate.

**They cannot prevent future data collection.** Once you are removed from a broker's database, they will likely re-acquire your data from public records updates within months. Ongoing subscriptions address this, but there is no permanent fix.

**They cannot help with data breaches.** If your email and password appeared in a breach, a removal service will not remove that data from dark web forums or breach repositories.

## The Bigger Picture: Why Data Broker Removal Matters

Data brokers are a largely invisible industry — there are 200–300+ companies in the US whose primary business is aggregating, buying, and selling personal information about private individuals. This includes people search sites (Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified), marketing databases (Acxiom, Epsilon), background check services, and financial data aggregators.

The information they hold is used to:
- Enable targeted advertising and junk mail
- Power background checks (some legitimate, some not)
- Facilitate identity theft and social engineering attacks
- Enable stalking and harassment (particularly concerning for domestic abuse survivors and public figures)
- Drive insurance pricing and credit decisions

Having your personal data available to anyone willing to pay $5 for a background check report is a genuine privacy and safety concern — not paranoia. Both services I tested are legitimate responses to a real problem.

## How I Verified Removals Independently

One thing I want to be transparent about: I did not just rely on the service dashboards to verify removal. I cross-checked results using three methods.

**Google search:** Weekly incognito searches for my name, phone number, and address. When Google stopped surfacing a specific broker listing, I counted that as verified removal from a practical privacy standpoint.

**Manual site checks:** For the 20 most important brokers, I visited the site directly each week and ran a search for my name. I checked whether my listing appeared at all, not just whether the service claimed it was removed.

**Google Alerts:** I set up Google Alerts for my name and address combination to catch any new or returned listings that appeared in Google's index.

At the 8-week mark, this independent verification was important. There were 7 cases where Incogni marked a removal "confirmed" but my manual check showed my listing was still accessible on that site — apparently in a state where the broker had acknowledged the request but not yet removed the listing. Incogni's automation eventually updated the status, but there was a lag.

PrivacyBee had 4 cases where their "confirmed removal" labels were ahead of actual site removal. Their human-assisted follow-up eventually caught these, but neither service is perfect.

**The lesson:** Service dashboards are useful approximations, not exact measures. The best way to know if you have been removed from a specific site is to check that site directly.

## What Happens To Your Data When You Cancel

Both services handle cancellation differently, and it is worth understanding before you sign up.

**If you cancel Incogni:**

Your removal requests that have already been confirmed remain in effect — data brokers that have deleted your information will not automatically reinstate it. However, Incogni stops monitoring for re-listing. Over the following 3–6 months, most brokers will re-acquire and re-publish your data from public records updates. Within six months of cancellation, your data footprint will likely return to something close to its pre-Incogni state on most brokers.

**If you cancel PrivacyBee:**

Same dynamic. Confirmed removals stay removed initially, but without ongoing monitoring and re-submission, re-listing is inevitable.

**The practical implication:** Data broker removal is a subscription service in the truest sense — the value is in the ongoing protection, not a one-time result. If you cancel, you lose the protection. This is not unique to either service; it is inherent to how data brokers operate.

If you want to cancel for budget reasons but maintain some protection, the most important thing to do before canceling is run a final pass of manual opt-outs on the five most important sites: Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified, MyLife, and Radaris. These five have the most traffic and direct impact on what people find when they Google your name.
