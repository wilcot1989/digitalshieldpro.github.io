You did not fill out a form to get listed on Spokeo. You did not agree to appear on Whitepages or BeenVerified. You did not give your home address to any of the dozens of people search sites that are currently displaying it to anyone who searches your name.

Yet there you are.

Understanding how your data got there — and why it keeps coming back after you remove it — requires understanding the mechanics of the data broker industry. This is a guide to exactly that: the seven primary collection methods data brokers use, how they combine data from multiple sources into detailed profiles, and what each method means for your privacy.

## The Scale of the Problem

Before getting into mechanics, it helps to understand the scale. There are an estimated 200–350 data broker companies in the United States. The largest — Acxiom, LexisNexis, Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion — hold comprehensive records on virtually every adult American. Acxiom alone claims to hold 10,000 attributes on 700 million people globally.

The industry generates over $200 billion annually. The vast majority of this revenue comes from B2B sales: marketers buying audience targeting data, insurers buying risk assessment data, employers buying background check data, financial institutions buying fraud detection data. People search sites (the ones you find when you Google yourself) are the consumer-facing tip of a massive commercial data ecosystem.

When you look yourself up on Spokeo and see your home address, phone number, and the names of your relatives displayed for anyone to view — that is the output of a sophisticated, industrial-scale data collection operation that has been running for decades.

## Source 1: Public Records

The largest single source of data broker information is government records. In the United States, an enormous amount of personal information is collected by government agencies and is legally classified as public — meaning anyone can access it.

**Property records:** Every real estate transaction is recorded by the county recorder's office and is publicly accessible. This includes: buyer and seller names, purchase price, property address, mailing address of the buyer, and in many cases the buyer's phone number if provided. When you buy or sell a home, your name and address are permanently linked in public records.

**Voter registration:** Most states make voter registration data available to political parties, researchers, and in some cases the general public. This data typically includes: full name, residential address, party affiliation (in primary-election states), and date of birth. California, for example, makes voter file data available to political parties and their representatives. Several other states sell or provide this data to requesters.

**Court records:** Court filings are public documents. If you have ever been involved in a lawsuit, criminal proceeding, divorce, bankruptcy, or probate case, those records include your name, address, and the details of the case. Many court systems have moved to electronic filing, making this data easier to access and aggregate at scale.

**Business registrations:** If you have ever formed an LLC, incorporated a business, or obtained a professional license, those registrations typically include your name and address. Business filings with the Secretary of State's office are public in every US state.

**Motor vehicle records:** The Driver's Privacy Protection Act (DPPA) restricts access to DMV records for most purposes, but permits access for a long list of exceptions including background checks, insurance, and certain research purposes. Some states have been criticized for selling DMV data to data brokers under these exceptions.

**Professional licenses:** Doctors, lawyers, contractors, real estate agents, and most other licensed professionals appear in state licensing databases that include their name, contact information, and sometimes home address.

**What this means:** A significant amount of the information on your data broker profile came from you interacting with government systems in the normal course of life. Buying a house. Registering to vote. Getting a professional license. There was no way to avoid this data collection while participating normally in society.

## Source 2: Credit Bureau Data and Financial Records

The three major credit bureaus — Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion — maintain detailed financial profiles on virtually every American adult. While credit score information is strictly regulated under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), non-FCRA data from these companies is available for other uses.

Credit bureau data includes:
- Names and aliases
- Current and historical addresses
- Employer names and employment history
- Accounts opened and closed
- Payment history
- Public records (bankruptcies, tax liens, civil judgments)

Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion all have data broker divisions that sell non-credit information derived from their databases. Experian Marketing Services, for example, sells consumer profiles to marketers that include demographic information, purchasing behavior, and financial status estimates derived from credit data. This is legal under FCRA as long as the data is not used for credit, insurance, or employment decisions.

When a data broker shows your "estimated income" or "financial status," that estimate is typically derived from census data, property values in your neighborhood, credit activity signals, and marketing database information — not your actual financial records.

## Source 3: Data Purchases and Data Reselling

Data brokers do not only collect data themselves — they buy it from each other and from commercial partners. This creates a secondary market where data flows continuously between companies, accumulating into increasingly comprehensive profiles.

**Retailers and loyalty programs:** Every time you swipe a loyalty card, make an online purchase, or use a store app, the retailer captures your purchase behavior. This data — what you buy, how often, how much you spend — is extremely valuable for marketing profiling. Many retailers sell this data to marketing data brokers or share it with partners.

**Financial service companies:** Credit card companies have detailed purchase data. Some sell aggregated and anonymized versions; others are known to share more with affiliates and data partners than their privacy policies clearly communicate.

**Telemarketing lists:** Companies collect email addresses, phone numbers, and mailing addresses through contests, newsletter signups, product registrations, and online forms. These lists are frequently sold to data brokers and list aggregators.

**Magazine subscriptions and direct mail:** Legacy data brokers built their earliest databases from magazine subscription records and catalog mailing lists. This tradition continues — data from subscription services regularly finds its way into marketing databases.

**Data broker to data broker:** Perhaps the most important source for understanding why your data keeps reappearing is inter-broker sales. When you opt out of Spokeo, Spokeo suppresses your record. But Spokeo continues to purchase updates from other data brokers who have not received your opt-out request. When those updates include your address, Spokeo re-lists you. This daisy-chain of data reselling is why removal is never permanent.

## Source 4: Web Scraping and Social Media Data

Data brokers use automated software to scrape publicly accessible information from websites, social media platforms, and online directories.

**Social media profiles:** Public Facebook profiles, LinkedIn profiles, Twitter/X accounts, and Instagram accounts contain a wealth of information: name, employer, location (city or neighborhood), relationship status, profile photos, public posts, and connections. Data brokers scrape this information and incorporate it into profiles.

**Online directory listings:** Yellow Pages, Google Business Profile (for self-employed people), Yelp, Healthgrades, Avvo, and similar directories publish contact information that gets scraped and added to data broker databases.

**Forum and community sites:** Public posts on Reddit, neighborhood Facebook groups, and other community forums can be scraped. If you have ever posted your neighborhood name, mentioned your street, or shared personal details in a public forum, that information is potentially in a data broker database.

**News articles and press releases:** If your name appears in a news article, press release, or online publication, data brokers scrape and index this information. A mention in a local business journal article can add your employer, position, and community involvement to your profile.

**What data brokers cannot scrape:** Content behind login walls, private social media posts, encrypted communications, and content on sites that block scraper bots through robots.txt restrictions or technical countermeasures. However, even "private" platforms often share data through third-party API integrations that reach data brokers through other routes.

## Source 5: Mobile App SDKs

This is the collection method most people are unaware of, and arguably the most insidious.

When a developer builds a mobile app, they often integrate third-party SDKs — Software Development Kits — that add functionality to their app. Some SDKs are benign (analytics, crash reporting, payment processing). Others are data collection tools from advertising and data broker companies.

When you install an app that includes a data broker SDK, that SDK may collect:

**Location data:** Precise GPS coordinates from your phone's location sensor. Apps that request location access "while using" or "always" can provide continuous location tracking if they integrate a data collection SDK. This generates a detailed record of where you live (where your phone spends nights), where you work (where it spends weekdays), and everywhere you go.

**Device identifiers:** Your phone's advertising identifier (IDFA on iOS, GAID on Android) links your activity across apps. Your device fingerprint (screen size, battery level patterns, installed apps) can identify your device even if you reset the advertising ID.

**Behavioral data:** What you tap, how long you use certain features, what content you engage with, what searches you perform within the app.

**Ambient data:** Some SDKs have been caught activating the microphone or camera in ways users did not anticipate. Apple and Google have added privacy restrictions to limit this, but older Android devices and apps with certain permissions can still access these sensors.

The apps most likely to include data collection SDKs are:
- Free games (mobile gaming is a primary revenue source for data SDK companies)
- Weather apps (location access = continuous location data)
- Coupon and deal apps
- Free VPN apps (ironic but well-documented)
- Utility apps (flashlights, battery monitors)
- Unofficial apps and apps from less-known developers

**Notable cases:** The New York Times documented a case where a supposedly anonymous mobility dataset sold by data brokers was successfully de-anonymized, revealing the precise movements of specific individuals including journalists and celebrities. The data came from SDKs embedded in apps those individuals had installed.

## Source 6: Data Breaches and Dark Web Purchases

The least reputable data brokers, and some more reputable ones, acquire data from breach databases. When a company is hacked and user data is stolen, that data often appears on dark web markets within days or weeks. Some data brokers — particularly those focused on fraud detection and risk assessment — purchase this breach data to enhance their profiles.

Breach data typically includes:
- Email addresses and passwords (hashed or plaintext)
- Names and addresses associated with account profiles
- Phone numbers linked to accounts
- Purchase history and account activity

Even reputable data brokers may have breach-derived data in their databases without knowing it — they purchase data from intermediaries who may not disclose the source. This is why data brokers sometimes have surprisingly accurate information about accounts or services you have never disclosed publicly.

**What this means for opt-outs:** If a data broker has your information from a breach database rather than a public records source, opting out removes the visible listing but does not necessarily remove the underlying data from their internal records. This is why CCPA deletion requests (which require deletion of data, not just opt-out of display) are more powerful than simple opt-out requests.

## Source 7: Voter and Political Data

Voter registration databases are one of the most significant public record sources for data brokers, and the accessibility of this data varies dramatically by state.

In states with permissive voter file access (several states in the South and Midwest sell voter file data to authorized requesters for a nominal fee), data brokers can access:
- Full legal name
- Residential address
- Mailing address
- Date of birth
- Party affiliation
- Voting history (which elections you voted in, not how you voted)
- Phone number (in states that collect it on voter registration forms)

In states with stricter voter file protection (California, New York, and others restrict access to credentialed researchers and political parties), this data is less accessible to commercial brokers.

This is why simply moving to a new address often causes your data to reappear on broker sites — when you re-register to vote at your new address, that registration update becomes publicly accessible within weeks in many states.

## How Data Brokers Combine Sources Into Profiles

The most important thing to understand is that data brokers do not simply collect individual data points — they combine information from multiple sources to build increasingly detailed profiles over time.

This process is called **identity resolution** or **record linkage**. Data brokers use probabilistic matching algorithms to determine that "James Mitchell at 123 Main St" and "J. Mitchell, 123 Main Street" and "James A. Mitchell" in three different databases are the same person.

Once the identity is confirmed, data from all sources is aggregated into a single profile. A name-and-address match from a voter file gets enriched with purchase behavior from a retail loyalty database, location history from a mobile SDK, court records from a public records aggregator, and social media profile data from web scraping. The result is a profile far more detailed than any single source would provide.

This aggregation is why the opt-out problem is persistent. Removing your name from one data broker's visible listing does not remove your data from the underlying identity resolution databases that feed multiple brokers. The next time any source updates your information, the re-listing process begins again.

## The Future of Data Broker Collection

The methods described in this guide are evolving. A few trends worth tracking:

**AI-powered inference.** Data brokers increasingly use machine learning to infer information that was not directly collected — inferring health conditions from purchase data, predicting creditworthiness from location and behavioral patterns, estimating political views from media consumption. These inferences often cannot be corrected through opt-out because they are derived data rather than collected facts.

**Biometric data.** Several states now have biometric privacy laws (Illinois BIPA is the strongest), but facial recognition data and voiceprints are increasingly valuable to data brokers and inadequately regulated at the federal level.

**Connected home and IoT devices.** Smart speakers, smart TVs, connected thermostats and appliances all generate behavioral data. The terms of service for these devices often include broad data sharing rights.

**V2X and connected vehicle data.** Modern vehicles transmit location, speed, driving behavior, and destination data. Automakers sell this data; insurance companies purchase it. As vehicles become more connected, this becomes an increasingly significant data broker source.

Understanding these emerging sources is important context for evaluating how much of your personal information exists in commercial databases — and why active management of your data broker footprint will remain relevant for the foreseeable future.

## Practical Implications: What You Can Actually Control

After reviewing all seven collection methods, it is useful to categorize them by how much individual action can reduce them:

**High control (you can meaningfully reduce these):**
- Social media scraping: Audit your privacy settings. Set location, employer, and relationship info to friends-only. Remove birthdate from public profiles.
- App SDK collection: Review app permissions. Delete apps that do not need location access. Use iOS and Android privacy settings to restrict tracking.
- Marketing list data: Opt out of DMAchoice.org, the national direct mail opt-out. Unsubscribe from marketing emails aggressively. Opt out of data sharing in loyalty program settings.

**Moderate control (you can reduce but not eliminate):**
- Social media data purchases: You can limit what platforms share by reviewing third-party app connections, but the platform's own data deals are outside your control.
- Inter-broker reselling: Removal requests at one broker reduce the data available for resale, but cannot prevent all re-acquisition.

**Low control (these are hard to avoid without major lifestyle changes):**
- Public records: Most public records are generated automatically when you interact with government — voting, driving, buying property, paying taxes. You can minimize some inputs (using PO Box addresses, for example) but cannot opt out entirely.
- Credit bureau data: FCRA governs what can be done with this data, but the collection itself is inherent to financial participation.
- Breach data: Once your information appears in a breach, it is in adversarial databases that you cannot remove yourself from through any opt-out mechanism.

This categorization is useful for prioritizing effort. Spend your time on the high-control items, use removal services for the moderate-control items, and accept that low-control items require different mitigations (fraud monitoring, strong passwords, security keys).

## How Data Brokers Make Money: The Business Model

Understanding the collection methods is more meaningful when you understand what motivates the industry. Data brokers are not collecting your information out of curiosity — they are building a product that sells for substantial revenue.

**Marketing data:** The largest segment. Advertisers and direct marketers pay for audience data: lists of people who match specific demographic profiles, have specific interests, have recently made relevant purchases, or live in target geographies. Acxiom's annual revenue is over $1.4 billion, almost entirely from marketing data products.

**Risk assessment and fraud detection:** Financial institutions, insurers, and background check companies pay for data that helps them assess risk. Your financial history, address stability, court records, and other signals are packaged into risk scores sold to banks, insurance companies, and employers.

**Identity verification:** When you sign up for a financial service and they ask security questions or verify your identity, they are often pulling from a data broker database to generate the questions or verify your answers. LexisNexis and similar companies sell identity verification as a product.

**People search (consumer facing):** Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified, and similar sites are the retail consumer end of the industry. A user pays $5–30 for a background check report on another individual. This is a small fraction of overall industry revenue but the most visible part from a privacy standpoint.

**Real estate and financial intelligence:** Property data, ownership history, and wealth indicators are sold to real estate investors, financial advisors, and marketing companies targeting high-net-worth individuals.

The result is an industry with strong financial incentives to collect as much data as possible, resist regulation, and make opt-out processes as difficult as possible. This is not paranoia — it is the logical behavior of a profit-maximizing business operating in a regulatory environment that has historically given it wide latitude.
