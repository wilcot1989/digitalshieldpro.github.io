## What Data Brokers Know About You

Before you can remove your data, it helps to understand what these companies are collecting and from where.

### Categories of Data Collected

**Identity data:**
- Full name, common aliases
- Date of birth
- Current and previous addresses (with dates)
- Phone numbers (mobile and landline)
- Email addresses

**Social and relational data:**
- Family members and associates
- Neighbours
- Colleagues (from LinkedIn scraping)
- Social media connections

**Financial data:**
- Property ownership and purchase history
- Estimated income and wealth range
- Bankruptcy history
- Business ownership records

**Behavioural data:**
- Purchase history (from loyalty cards, retailer data partnerships)
- Browsing habits (from app permissions and tracking pixels)
- Lifestyle inferences (sports, health interests, dietary choices)
- Political and charitable donation history

**Sensitive inferences:**
- Health conditions (inferred from purchase data)
- Political leanings
- Religious affiliation
- Sexual orientation (inferred from behavioural patterns)

### Where They Get It

**Public records:** Electoral roll (in the UK, the edited register is legally saleable), Land Registry, Companies House, court records, birth/death/marriage registrations.

**Social media scraping:** Publicly visible profile information, posts, photos with location data.

**Commercial data purchases:** Retailers sell anonymized purchase data to brokers. "Anonymized" sets are frequently re-identified. Loyalty card schemes are a major source.

**Data partnerships:** Apps sell location data, browsing data, and usage statistics. A free flashlight app from 2019 may have sold your location data to 40 brokers.

**Inferential processing:** Brokers combine and cross-reference data from multiple sources to fill gaps and make predictions. They infer your income from your postcode and property value. They infer health conditions from pharmacy purchases.

## Your GDPR Rights — Explained Practically

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) applies across the EU. UK GDPR — the domesticated version post-Brexit — applies in the UK with near-identical provisions. Both give you the following relevant rights:

### Article 15: Right of Access

You can request confirmation of what personal data a company holds about you and receive a copy of it. This is your starting point — know what they have before requesting deletion.

**Practical use:** Submit a Subject Access Request (SAR) to a broker. They must respond within 30 days with all personal data they hold on you.

### Article 17: Right to Erasure (Right to Be Forgotten)

You can request deletion of your personal data when:
- The data is no longer necessary for the original purpose it was collected
- You withdraw consent (if consent was the basis for processing)
- You object to processing and there are no overriding legitimate grounds
- The data was unlawfully processed
- Deletion is required to comply with a legal obligation

**Key point for data brokers:** Many brokers process your data under "legitimate interest" as their legal basis. This is contestable — brokers selling your personal data to other brokers and marketers is not a clear-cut legitimate interest. Article 17 applies when you object and they cannot demonstrate compelling legitimate grounds.

### Article 21: Right to Object

You can object to processing at any time when the legal basis is legitimate interest or a public task. Upon objection, the data controller must stop processing unless they can demonstrate "compelling legitimate grounds which override the interests, rights and freedoms of the data subject."

For commercial data brokers, this test is very difficult to meet. When you object, they should stop processing.

**Practical use:** Send an Article 21 objection simultaneously with your Article 17 erasure request. This creates dual grounds for removal.

### Article 16: Right to Rectification

You can request correction of inaccurate data. While this does not remove your data, it is useful when brokers hold wrong information that could affect insurance quotes, credit decisions, or other outcomes.

## Step-by-Step Manual Removal Process

### Step 1: Discover Your Data

Before submitting removal requests, audit what brokers have.

**EU/UK-focused data brokers to check:**
- Spokeo.com (US-heavy but has EU data)
- PeekYou.com
- Whitepages.com
- AnyWho.com
- BeenVerified.com
- Intelius.com
- MyLife.com
- Pipl.com
- Zabasearch.com

**UK-specific:**
- 192.com (large UK directory)
- BT Phone Book
- Royal Mail address database (check business directories built on it)
- Yell.com
- Thomson Local

**EU directory sites (check in your country):**
- TrueCallers (directory)
- National telephone directories (e.g., Pages Blanches in France, Telefonbuch in Germany)

Search using combinations of: your name + town, your name + old address, your name + employer.

### Step 2: Identify the Data Controller

For GDPR requests, you need to identify who is responsible for your data. Data brokers must list:
- Their company name and registration
- A Data Protection Officer (DPO) contact for requests
- Their privacy policy with erasure request instructions

Find this in the site's Privacy Policy > "Your Rights" section, or their GDPR/privacy contact page.

### Step 3: Draft an Effective Erasure Request

An effective request should be concise and formally invoke your GDPR rights. Here is a template:

---

*Subject: GDPR Right to Erasure Request — [Your Name]*

Dear Data Protection Officer,

I am writing to exercise my rights under the General Data Protection Regulation (EU 2016/679) [or UK GDPR].

I request the immediate erasure of all personal data you hold relating to me, including but not limited to: [name, address, phone number, email address, associated family members].

I rely on Article 17 (Right to Erasure) and Article 21 (Right to Object to Processing) in making this request. As a data broker, your legitimate interest claim for processing my personal data for commercial sale does not override my fundamental right to privacy under Article 8 of the EU Charter.

I require:
1. Confirmation that all personal data relating to me has been erased
2. Confirmation that my data will not be re-acquired and re-processed
3. Details of any third parties to whom you have disclosed my data (so I can exercise rights with them directly)

Please respond within the 30-day statutory period.

My details for identification: [Full name, date of birth, current address, previous address if applicable]

[Your name]

---

### Step 4: Send the Request

Send by email with read receipt if possible. If no email address is available, send via:
- Their online privacy request form (document with screenshots)
- Postal mail to their registered UK/EU address (send recorded delivery)

Keep records of everything — date sent, method, who responded, and what they said.

### Step 5: Follow Up and Escalate

If you receive no response within 30 days:

**UK:** Escalate to the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) — ico.org.uk — Report a concern form. The ICO can investigate and fine companies for GDPR violations.

**EU:** Escalate to your national supervisory authority. A complete list is at edpb.europa.eu/about-edpb/about-edpb/members_en.

When escalating, include:
- Your original request (with date)
- Evidence it was received (email delivery confirmation, postal tracking)
- That you have not received a response

## The Problem With Manual Removal

I submitted manual erasure requests to 30 data brokers as part of my January 2026 audit. Here is what happened:

- 12 responded within the statutory 30-day period
- 8 responded late (between 30-60 days)
- 6 sent automated rejection responses citing "legitimate interest"
- 4 did not respond at all
- Time invested: approximately 15 hours across discovery, request drafting, and follow-up

Three months later, I checked again. 11 of the 20 brokers that had complied had re-listed me, having acquired my data again from another source.

This is the fundamental problem with manual removal: it is a one-time intervention against a continuous process. Brokers continuously acquire new data and re-list previously deleted entries.

## Additional EU-Specific Removal Tools

### Google Personal Information Removal

Google allows removal requests for personal contact information (address, phone, email) appearing in search results under specific conditions.

Visit: myaccount.google.com/delete-services-or-account — and Google Search > "Results about you" feature.

The "Results about you" tool (available in most EU countries) actively monitors Google search results for your personal information and allows you to request removal with a few clicks.

### Right to Be Forgotten in Search Results

EU residents can request removal of specific search results under the Right to Be Forgotten established by the CJEU. This does not remove the underlying page — only the search result.

Submit at: google.com/webmasters/tools/legal-removal-requests

Google evaluates each request on balance of public interest. Private individuals with no public role have strong grounds for removal.

### National Do Not Call Registries

**UK:** Telephone Preference Service — tpsonline.org.uk (landline and mobile)
**Germany:** No formal national registry; opt-out through individual companies
**Netherlands:** Register at infofilter.nl for mail and telefoonregister.nl for calls
**France:** Bloctel — bloctel.gouv.fr

These registries suppress some commercial calls but do not affect data broker databases directly.

## Building a Long-Term Data Minimisation Strategy

Removal is a reactive measure. Minimising future data collection reduces the ongoing burden.

### Reduce Future Data Collection

- **Loyalty cards:** Use cash where possible or dedicated loyalty-card email addresses that are not your primary address
- **App permissions:** Regularly audit location, contacts, and advertising ID permissions on your phone
- **Electoral roll:** In the UK, you can opt out of the edited register (sold to brokers) while remaining on the full register (used for elections). Contact your local council.
- **Social media:** Set profiles to private; audit what is publicly visible; remove historical location data from posts

### Use Data-Protective Services

- Privacy-focused email (Proton Mail) for registrations to reduce your real email appearing in broker databases
- A VoIP secondary phone number (Google Voice, Skype) for merchant registrations
- A PO box or mail forwarding address for physical mail registrations

### Regular Audits

Every 6 months, repeat the initial search across major brokers. New brokers emerge regularly, and existing ones re-list. Combined with an active Incogni subscription, this keeps your exposure minimal.

## Escalation: Filing GDPR Complaints

If a data broker refuses your request without adequate justification, here is the complaint process.

### ICO (UK)

1. Visit ico.org.uk/make-a-complaint
2. Select "Data protection complaint"
3. Provide: the company name, your request details, their response (or lack thereof), and why you believe they are non-compliant
4. The ICO will assess and may open an investigation

ICO enforcement action can result in fines up to £17.5 million or 4% of global turnover (whichever is higher) for serious violations.

### EU National Authorities

File with the authority in your country of residence (not where the company is based — GDPR one-stop-shop applies to companies, not individuals). For complaints about companies headquartered in Ireland (e.g., Facebook, Google), you file with the Irish DPC, but for data brokers in other countries you file with your national authority.
