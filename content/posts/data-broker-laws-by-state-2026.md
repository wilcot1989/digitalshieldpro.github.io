## The Federal Baseline: What Protects Everyone (Sort Of)

Before diving into states, it is worth understanding what federal law does and does not cover for data broker issues.

### Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)

The FCRA, passed in 1970 and updated multiple times since, is the primary federal law governing a subset of data broker activity. It covers "consumer reporting agencies" — companies that assemble or evaluate consumer information for use in:
- Credit decisions
- Employment background checks
- Insurance underwriting
- Tenant screening
- Other "eligibility determinations"

Under FCRA, you have the right to:
- See your consumer report (free annually)
- Dispute inaccurate information
- Know when your report has been used against you
- Have most negative information removed after 7 years (10 for bankruptcy)

**What FCRA does not cover:** Data brokers that are not producing reports for these specific purposes. The people search sites (Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified) actively try to structure their services to avoid FCRA classification, which would impose compliance burdens. Most general data brokers do not fall under FCRA.

### CAN-SPAM and TCPA

The CAN-SPAM Act gives you the right to opt out of commercial email marketing. The Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) restricts robocalls and requires consent for automated marketing calls. Neither law directly addresses data broker retention or deletion, but they provide rights against the uses data brokers enable.

### FTC Authority

The FTC has authority to take action against unfair or deceptive trade practices by data brokers, and has done so in notable cases. However, the FTC's enforcement is case-by-case and reactive — it does not give individual consumers proactive deletion rights.

## California: The Gold Standard

California has the strongest comprehensive consumer privacy law in the United States. The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), passed in 2018 and effective January 2020, was amended by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA) in 2020, which took effect in 2023 and created the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) to enforce it.

### Who Is Covered

CCPA applies to for-profit businesses that do business in California and meet any one of these thresholds:
- Annual gross revenue over $25 million
- Buy, sell, or receive personal information of 100,000+ California consumers or households per year
- Derive 50%+ of annual revenue from selling personal information

This covers virtually all significant data brokers.

### Your CCPA Rights

**Right to know:** You can request a data broker to disclose what personal information they have collected about you, where they got it, what they use it for, and whether they sell or share it.

**Right to delete:** You can request that a data broker delete your personal information. The broker must also instruct their service providers and contractors to delete it.

**Right to opt out of sale:** You can direct a business not to sell or share your personal information. Data brokers must provide a "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" link on their website.

**Right to correct:** You can request that a data broker correct inaccurate personal information they hold.

**Right to limit use of sensitive information:** For sensitive data (Social Security numbers, financial account data, precise geolocation, racial or ethnic origin, etc.), you can limit use beyond what is necessary for providing the service.

**Right to non-discrimination:** Businesses cannot discriminate against you for exercising these rights.

### California Data Broker Registry

Under CPRA amendments, California created a data broker registry where data brokers must register annually with the CPPA and provide information about their data practices. The registry is publicly searchable at cppa.ca.gov/data-broker-registry.

Starting in 2026, data brokers registered in California must also allow consumers to submit deletion requests through a standardized process — and through an authorized agent, which is how automated services like Incogni operate on your behalf.

### How to Exercise California CCPA Rights

**Option 1: Direct broker contact**
Most major data brokers have CCPA request portals on their websites. Search for "[broker name] privacy request" or look for a "Do Not Sell My Personal Information" link in their footer. Submit a deletion request through their portal.

**Option 2: Global Privacy Control**
The Global Privacy Control (GPC) is a browser signal that communicates your opt-out preference to websites automatically. Installing a GPC-enabled browser extension (Privacy Badger, for example, supports it) sends an automatic opt-out signal to participating websites. California law requires businesses that recognize the GPC signal to treat it as a valid opt-out.

**Option 3: Authorized agent**
Services like Incogni act as your "authorized agent" under CCPA — meaning they submit legally binding deletion requests on your behalf. Under CCPA, brokers must comply with requests submitted by authorized agents with your documented authorization.

**Response time:** Data brokers must respond to CCPA requests within 45 days (extendable to 90 days with notice).

## Texas: The Second Major Law

Texas's Texas Data Privacy and Security Act (TDPSA) went into effect July 1, 2024. Texas is the second-largest state economy in the US, making TDPSA significant for the industry even though California CCPA is more comprehensive.

### Who Is Covered

TDPSA applies to any person who:
- Conducts business in Texas or produces products or services consumed by Texas residents
- Processes personal data above certain thresholds (excluding businesses under 25 employees for some provisions)

Unlike CCPA, TDPSA does not have a specific revenue threshold — it applies more broadly.

### Your TDPSA Rights

**Right to access:** Confirm whether a controller processes your personal data, and access your data.

**Right to correct:** Request correction of inaccurate personal data.

**Right to delete:** Request deletion of personal data you provided or that was collected about you.

**Right to portability:** Obtain a copy of your personal data in a portable format.

**Right to opt out of:**
- Sale of your personal data
- Targeted advertising
- Profiling decisions with legal or significant effects

**Sensitive data:** TDPSA requires opt-in consent for processing sensitive personal data, including precise geolocation, racial/ethnic origin, health data, and sexual orientation.

### Texas-Specific Provisions

**No private right of action:** Unlike California, Texas consumers cannot sue data brokers directly under TDPSA. Enforcement is by the Texas Attorney General only. This is a meaningful limitation — brokers face less compliance pressure from individual consumers in Texas than in California.

**Universal opt-out mechanisms:** Texas recognizes universal opt-out mechanisms (like Global Privacy Control) that allow browsers to automatically signal opt-out preferences.

**Data broker registration:** The TDPSA has provisions specifically addressing data brokers. Companies operating as data brokers in Texas must register with the state.

### How to Exercise Texas TDPSA Rights

**Direct requests:** Submit deletion requests directly to data broker websites via their privacy portals. Texas residents can cite TDPSA as the legal basis for their request.

**AG complaints:** If a data broker fails to respond or denies your request improperly, file a complaint with the Texas Office of the Attorney General at texasattorneygeneral.gov.

**Authorized agents:** As in California, Texas allows authorized agents to submit requests on your behalf. Incogni and similar services can operate as your authorized agent.

## Vermont: The Data Broker Registry State

Vermont's approach to data broker regulation is different from California or Texas. Rather than creating broad consumer privacy rights, Vermont's H.764 (2018) created a specific data broker registration requirement.

### What Vermont's Law Does

**Data broker registration:** Any business that "knowingly collects and sells or licenses to third parties the brokered personal information of a Vermont consumer" must:
- Register with the Vermont Secretary of State annually
- Pay a $100 annual registration fee
- Disclose their data collection and opt-out practices

The registry is public and searchable at sos.vermont.gov/data-brokers.

**Opt-out requirement:** Registered data brokers must provide Vermont consumers with a "clear and conspicuous means to opt out" of having their personal information sold.

**Security and transparency requirements:** Registered brokers must disclose:
- What types of data they collect
- Whether they allow consumers to opt out of data collection
- Whether they implement security programs
- Whether they restrict certain uses

### What Vermont's Law Does NOT Do

Vermont's law does not give consumers a right to:
- Request deletion of their data (only opt-out of future sale)
- Know exactly what data the broker holds about them
- Correct inaccurate data
- Sue the broker directly for violations

The law is primarily a transparency measure. Its value is that consumers can look up whether a company is operating as a data broker, which is useful context even if direct rights are limited.

### The Vermont Registry in Practice

The Vermont registry currently lists 150+ registered data brokers. Searching the registry for names like Acxiom, LexisNexis, Spokeo, or BeenVerified confirms they are operating and shows their stated opt-out mechanisms. This is a useful starting point for identifying who to contact.

## Virginia: Comprehensive Rights Without a Broker Registry

Virginia's Consumer Data Protection Act (CDPA) took effect January 1, 2023. Virginia was one of the first states to pass comprehensive privacy legislation following California.

### Your Virginia CDPA Rights

Virginia residents have rights similar to CCPA but with important differences:

**Rights available:**
- Access your personal data
- Correct inaccurate personal data
- Delete personal data
- Obtain a portable copy
- Opt out of targeted advertising, sale, and profiling

**Right to appeal:** Unique to Virginia — if a business denies your privacy request, you have the right to appeal that decision to the controller, and then to the Virginia Attorney General if the appeal is denied.

**No private right of action:** Like Texas, Virginia does not allow individual consumers to sue under CDPA. Enforcement is by the Virginia AG only.

**No data broker registry:** Unlike Vermont and California, Virginia does not have a specific data broker registration requirement.

### Practical Impact for Virginians

Submit deletion requests to data brokers citing CDPA as the legal basis. Data brokers must respond within 45 days. If denied, you have an appeal path through the AG. The lack of a private right of action means enforcement pressure is lower than in California, but the rights themselves are comparable.

## Colorado, Connecticut, and Montana: The Second Wave

Several additional states have passed comprehensive privacy laws that include data broker obligations:

### Colorado Privacy Act (CPA)

Effective July 1, 2023. Rights: access, correction, deletion, portability, opt-out of sale and targeted advertising. Universal opt-out mechanism required. 45-day response period. No private right of action.

### Connecticut Data Privacy Act (CTDPA)

Effective July 1, 2023. Similar rights to Colorado. Notable: includes a right to opt out through browser-based universal opt-out signals (like Global Privacy Control) which the controller must honor.

### Montana Consumer Data Privacy Act (MCDPA)

Effective October 1, 2024. Access, correction, deletion, portability, opt-out rights. Montana's law has some of the broadest exemptions of the second-wave states.

### Iowa Consumer Data Protection Act

Effective January 1, 2025. More limited than other states — no right to correct data, no right to opt out of profiling. Primarily opt-out of sale and targeted advertising.

## States With No Comprehensive Privacy Law: What Applies

If you live in a state not listed above — which covers most of the US population — your data broker rights are limited to:

**FCRA rights:** If the data broker produces consumer reports, you have FCRA access and dispute rights.

**FTC:** The FTC can take action against deceptive practices. Filing an FTC complaint at ftc.gov/complaint can trigger enforcement attention, though individual relief is rare.

**Direct broker opt-outs:** Most major data brokers provide opt-out mechanisms that are accessible to all users regardless of state, often because complying with state-by-state residency verification is too complex.

**Practical approach:** Even without state privacy rights, submit opt-out requests directly to data broker sites. Most brokers process requests from all users, not just those from protected states, because the cost of verifying state residency exceeds the cost of processing the request.

## Practical Guide: Exercising Your Rights By State

### For California Residents

1. **Use Global Privacy Control:** Install a GPC-enabled browser extension (Privacy Badger, DuckDuckGo Browser, etc.) to automatically signal opt-out to participating sites.

2. **Submit CCPA deletion requests:** Use data broker privacy portals or a service like Incogni as your authorized agent.

3. **Check the California data broker registry:** cppa.ca.gov/data-broker-registry — find registered brokers and their opt-out mechanisms.

4. **File complaints with CPPA:** If a broker fails to comply, file at cppa.ca.gov.

### For Texas Residents

1. **Submit deletion requests** citing TDPSA to data broker privacy portals.

2. **Use universal opt-out mechanisms** — Texas recognizes GPC.

3. **File AG complaints** for non-compliance at texasattorneygeneral.gov.

### For Virginia, Colorado, Connecticut, Montana Residents

1. **Submit deletion requests** citing your state's applicable law (CDPA, CPA, CTDPA, or MCDPA).

2. **Use authorized agent services** (Incogni, DeleteMe, Optery) — these services submit legally formatted requests citing the applicable state law.

3. **Appeal denials** through the AG process available in your state.

### For Vermont Residents

1. **Check the Vermont data broker registry** at sos.vermont.gov/data-brokers to identify registered brokers.

2. **Submit opt-out requests** to registered brokers using the mechanisms they disclose.

3. **File complaints** with the Vermont AG for brokers who fail to provide required opt-out mechanisms.

### For All Other States

1. **Submit opt-out requests directly** — most major brokers process requests from all users.

2. **Use FCRA rights** for brokers producing consumer reports.

3. **File FTC complaints** for deceptive practices at ftc.gov/complaint.

## The Federal Landscape: What Congress Has (Not) Done

Congress has repeatedly introduced federal privacy legislation that would create national standards for data broker rights. As of mid-2026, no comprehensive federal privacy law has passed.

The American Data Privacy and Protection Act (ADPPA) was advanced by the House Commerce Committee in 2022 but stalled in the full House. Modified versions have been introduced in subsequent sessions without passing. The political obstacles are familiar: tension between federal preemption (would override state laws, which California opposes) and the desire for uniform national standards.

Without federal legislation, the patchwork of state laws will continue to grow. The practical result is that Americans in California, Texas, and a dozen other states have meaningful data broker rights, while residents of states like Alabama, Georgia, and Ohio have few enforceable protections beyond FCRA.

## How to File a Complaint When a Broker Does Not Comply

Knowing your rights is only useful if you can enforce them. Here is a practical guide to filing complaints when data brokers ignore or improperly deny deletion requests.

### California: CPPA Complaints

The California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) is the enforcement body for CCPA/CPRA violations.

**When to file:** If a data broker operating in California does not respond to your deletion request within 45 days (or 90 days with proper notice), denies your request without a valid legal basis, or fails to instruct service providers to delete your data after confirming deletion.

**How to file:** Go to cppa.ca.gov and use their consumer complaint portal. Provide: the broker's name, your request date, the response received (or lack thereof), and any documentation you have.

**What to expect:** The CPPA investigates patterns of violations rather than individual cases, but consistent complaints about specific brokers do drive enforcement attention. Several brokers have been contacted or investigated following complaint volume.

### Texas: AG Consumer Protection

**When to file:** Same criteria as California — non-response, improper denial, or non-compliance by a broker operating in Texas.

**How to file:** texasattorneygeneral.gov → Consumer Protection → File a Complaint.

**Note:** Texas has no private right of action, so AG complaints are your primary enforcement avenue.

### Federal: FTC Complaints

For brokers not subject to specific state laws, or for patterns of deceptive practices:

**How to file:** ftc.gov/complaint

The FTC tracks complaint patterns and pursues enforcement against companies with significant violation volumes. Individual relief is rare, but collective complaint patterns influence enforcement priorities.

### Virginia: AG Consumer Protection

Virginia residents who receive an improper denial can appeal first to the data broker directly, then to the Virginia AG at ag.virginia.gov/Consumer-Protection.
