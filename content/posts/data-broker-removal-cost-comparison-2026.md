## The True Cost Framework

Before comparing services, we need to agree on what "cost" actually means. For data broker removal, there are three cost components:

1. **Direct cost:** What you pay in dollars per year
2. **Time cost:** Hours spent on removal × your hourly time value
3. **Coverage cost:** Gaps in broker coverage that leave your data exposed

I will calculate all three for each approach. For time valuation, I will use $25/hour — below US median wage but appropriate for a tedious administrative task most people would rather not do.

## Option 1: DIY Manual Removal

### What DIY Involves

Manual data broker removal means visiting each broker's opt-out page individually, submitting your information, confirming via email or phone, and tracking your progress. Then doing it all again every 3–6 months when your data re-appears.

### Time Calculation: Initial Round

| Task | Estimated Time |
|------|---------------|
| Research: finding all relevant brokers | 2–3 hours |
| Locating your listing on each site | 0.5–1 min per site × 200 sites = 1.5–3 hours |
| Completing each opt-out form | 5–15 min per site × 200 sites = 17–50 hours |
| Email confirmations and follow-up | 1–2 hours |
| Verification checks | 2–4 hours |
| **Initial total** | **22–58 hours** |

That range is wide because broker opt-out processes vary enormously. Spokeo takes 10 minutes. MyLife took me 45 minutes and required creating an account. LexisNexis required ID upload. Some brokers require physical mail.

For a realistic estimate, I will use 35 hours for the initial round.

### Time Calculation: Ongoing Maintenance

Data re-appears. Brokers continuously pull from public records updates — when you move, renew a vehicle registration, vote, or appear in any public filing. Realistically:

- Monthly monitoring: 1 hour/month = 12 hours/year
- Quarterly re-submission rounds: 10 hours × 4 = 40 hours/year
- **Annual maintenance: ~52 hours/year after the initial round**

### DIY True Annual Cost

| Component | Year 1 | Year 2+ |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Direct cost | $0 | $0 |
| Time (hours) | 87 hours | 52 hours |
| Time cost at $25/hr | $2,175 | $1,300 |
| **Total true cost** | **$2,175** | **$1,300/year** |

DIY data broker removal is extremely expensive in time. Most people dramatically underestimate this when they think "I'll just do it myself."

## What These Numbers Actually Mean

The most important insight from this analysis: **DIY removal is the most expensive option by a large margin when you account for time.** This surprises most people because the direct dollar cost is zero. But if you value your time at all, automated services deliver dramatically better value.

Among paid services:

**Incogni delivers the best price-to-coverage ratio.** At $77.88/year, it handles 170+ brokers automatically with minimal time investment. The $203 true annual cost is the lowest of any paid service.

**DeleteMe justifies its premium with human verification.** The $229 true annual cost is close to Incogni, but DeleteMe includes quarterly PDF reports with human-verified removal confirmations — a significant quality assurance improvement for people who want certainty rather than automated status labels.

**Optery Ultimate is worth considering if you want maximum broker coverage.** At 500+ brokers, it covers significantly more ground than Incogni. The true annual cost is higher, but if you are concerned about small and obscure brokers in addition to the majors, Optery's breadth is unmatched at its price point.

**PrivacyBee makes sense for specific high-risk situations.** If you are a public figure, executive, domestic abuse survivor, or have specific security concerns, the human-assisted follow-up is worth the premium. For average consumers, it is expensive for what you get.

## The Case Against Doing Nothing

One option I have not analyzed: doing nothing. Many people know their data is out there but assume there is not much they can do, or that the risk is not real.

The risk is real. People search sites are actively used by:
- Stalkers and harassers (the majority of restraining orders now include data broker listings)
- Phishing attackers who use personal data to craft convincing social engineering attacks
- Identity thieves who combine public records with breach data
- Robocallers and spam marketers who purchase lists from data brokers

Your home address, phone number, and relative names being freely available online is a concrete security and safety risk, not a theoretical one.
