## Understanding Why Elderly People Are Targeted

Before getting into setup steps, it is worth understanding why older adults are disproportionately targeted. This knowledge shapes how we approach protection.

### Financial and Social Factors

According to FBI IC3 data, adults over 60 lose more money to cybercrime than any other age group. The 2024 IC3 Elder Fraud Report documented $3.4 billion in losses from over 101,000 complaints — an average of nearly $34,000 per victim.

Reasons older adults are targeted:
- **Accumulated wealth**: Retirement savings make them high-value targets
- **Social isolation**: Loneliness makes them more responsive to friendly contact, including from scammers
- **Trust in authority**: Older generations were raised to respect authority figures — police, banks, government agencies. Scammers exploit this.
- **Unfamiliarity with social engineering tactics**: Many older adults simply have not encountered the specific manipulation techniques scammers use
- **Less experience with tech fraud patterns**: New threats (AI voice cloning, deepfakes) are particularly unfamiliar

### New Threats in 2026

**AI voice cloning scams**: Using publicly available audio from social media (even a few seconds of video is enough), scammers clone a family member's voice and call the target. The "grandchild" sounds exactly right. They claim to be in jail, in a hospital, or in danger abroad, and request emergency wire transfers. These calls are highly convincing and often evade traditional "test question" defences because the cloned voice can answer basic questions correctly using scraped information.

**Deepfake video calls**: Emerging threat where scammers use real-time deepfake video to impersonate financial advisors or family members on video calls.

**WhatsApp account hijacking**: Scammer convinces target to forward a 6-digit code (actually a WhatsApp verification code), gaining access to the account and using it to scam the victim's contacts.

## Phase 1: The Conversation — Before Any Setup

The most important step comes before touching any device. Have a direct, non-condescending conversation about scams.

### How to Frame the Conversation

Do not frame it as "you are vulnerable and need protection." Frame it as "scams are incredibly sophisticated now and everyone is at risk — here is what I know."

Key points to cover:

**The gift card rule:** No legitimate business, government agency, police force, or family member will ever ask you to pay with gift cards. Ever. Gift cards are exclusively used by scammers because they are untraceable and non-refundable. If anyone — regardless of how official they sound — asks you to buy gift cards, it is a scam. Hang up.

**The Microsoft/Apple rule:** Microsoft and Apple do not cold-call customers. If you get a call claiming to be from technical support, it is a scam. If a pop-up on your screen has a phone number, do not call it — it is a scam. If your computer has a problem, call me (your family member) first.

**The urgency rule:** Scammers create panic. "Your account is about to be closed." "You owe back taxes and police are coming." "Your grandchild is in jail." Legitimate situations have time for you to make one phone call to a family member to verify. Always make that call before doing anything else.

**The remote access rule:** Never let anyone connect remotely to your computer unless you called them first. Once someone has remote access, they can see everything on your screen, install software, and steal passwords.

**The verification call rule:** If you get a call from your bank, HMRC, or any institution, hang up and call them back on the number printed on your card or their official website. Not the number the caller gives you.

### Create a "Scam Hotline" in the Family

Designate one family member as the person to call before doing anything requested by an unexpected contact. Make sure your elder has this number saved and accessible — on the fridge, as a phone contact, wherever they will find it quickly.

Establish the understanding: "I will never be too busy for this call. Call me any time before sending money or letting someone on your computer."

## Phase 2: Device Security Setup

Now for the practical setup. I will cover Windows (most common for older adults) and iPhone/Android.

### Windows PC Setup

#### Step 1: Create a Standard User Account

Most Windows users run as Administrator, meaning any malware they accidentally install gets full system access. Create a standard user account for daily use.

1. Settings > Accounts > Family & other users > Add someone else to this PC
2. Create a local account with a standard (not admin) user type
3. Set a password and store it in a safe place

The elder uses the standard account daily. The admin password stays with you.

#### Step 2: Enable Windows Defender and Keep It Updated

Ensure Windows Security is active:
- Windows Security > Virus & threat protection > Real-time protection: On
- Check for updates: Settings > Windows Update > Check for updates

Also enable:
- Windows Security > App & browser control > Smart App Control: On (Windows 11)
- Windows Security > Firewall & network protection: On for all networks

#### Step 4: Enable Automatic Updates

Settings > Windows Update > Advanced options:
- Automatic updates: On
- Receive updates for other Microsoft products: On
- Download updates over metered connections: Ask (to avoid surprise data charges)

#### Step 5: Set Up Browser Safety

For most older adults, Chrome or Edge is what they use. Set up safety features:

**Chrome:**
- Settings > Privacy and security > Safe Browsing: Enhanced protection
- Settings > Privacy and security > Always use secure connections: On

**Edge:**
- Settings > Privacy, search, and services > SmartScreen for Microsoft Edge: On
- Settings > Privacy, search, and services > Block potentially unwanted apps: On

Install uBlock Origin browser extension — a single extension that blocks ads, tracking scripts, and many phishing sites. It requires no management after installation.

#### Step 6: Configure Screen Lock

Require a password after 5 minutes of inactivity. Store the password on a physical note in a secure location at home (not a sticky note on the monitor).

### iPhone Setup for Elderly Users

#### Enable Screen Time Content & Privacy Restrictions

Counterintuitively, Screen Time is the best parental-control-style tool for protecting elderly iPhone users.

Settings > Screen Time > Content & Privacy Restrictions: On

Set a Screen Time passcode known only to you (the family member managing security). This prevents:
- New apps being installed without verification
- Web content restrictions being changed
- Location settings being altered

Useful restrictions:
- Web Content: Limit Adult Websites (also blocks many scam/phishing sites)
- iTunes & App Store Purchases: Require Password (prevents accidental purchases)

#### Enable Fraudulent Website Warning in Safari

Settings > Safari > Fraudulent Website Warning: On

#### Find My

Settings > [Name] > Find My > Find My iPhone: On. This is primarily for device recovery, but also helps if the device is taken or lost during a scam involving physical handoff.

#### Review Installed Apps

Go through all apps together. Delete anything not recognized or used. Scam apps occasionally appear even in the App Store.

### Android Setup

Android setups vary by manufacturer. The key steps:

1. Settings > Security > Screen Lock: Set PIN or pattern
2. Settings > Biometrics: Enable fingerprint for easy access
3. Google Play Protect: Play Store > Account icon > Play Protect > Ensure enabled
4. Settings > Privacy > Permission Manager: Review which apps have camera, microphone, contacts, and location access
5. Install Google Family Link or Bitdefender for parental-style protection

## Phase 3: Account Security

### Email Account

Email is the master key. With email access, an attacker can reset all other passwords.

**Steps:**
1. Create a strong unique password using a password manager or a memorable but unique passphrase
2. Enable 2-factor authentication — for elderly users, SMS 2FA (a code texted to their phone) is easier than authenticator apps
3. Enable login alerts: both Gmail and Outlook offer notifications when a new device logs in

For elderly users who are not technically confident, Gmail is the better choice — Google's security detection is industry-leading and flags unusual logins very effectively.

**Gmail-specific:** Google Account > Security > 2-Step Verification: On. Also check "Your devices" and "Recent security activity" together — remove any unrecognized devices.

### Social Media

Facebook is a major attack vector — both for direct scams targeting the elder and for account hijacking used to scam their contacts.

**Facebook security setup:**
1. Settings > Security and Login > Two-Factor Authentication: On (SMS)
2. Settings > Security and Login > Get alerts about unrecognized logins: On
3. Settings > Privacy > Who can send you friend requests: Friends of Friends (reduces unsolicited contact from scammers)
4. Settings > Privacy > Who can look you up using your email/phone: Friends

Discuss: Scammers create fake profiles of real people (friends, family, celebrities) and use them to make contact. Verify any new friend request that seems unusual with a phone call before accepting.

### Online Banking

Work with your elder to set up:
1. Login notifications for every transaction (text or email alert)
2. A spending limit or confirmation required for large transactions (many banks offer this)
3. Confirm which phone number is on file for 2FA — scammers try to change this

**Key message**: The bank will never call and ask for full account numbers, card PINs, or one-time passcodes. If anyone does this, hang up and call the bank's official number immediately.

## Phase 4: Physical and Practical Measures

### The Emergency Card

Create a laminated card for your elder's wallet with:
- "[Family member name] — call before doing anything involving money or computers: [phone number]"
- "Microsoft and Apple never call people. If this happened, it is a scam."
- "Gift cards are only used by scammers. Never buy them for payments."
- "No government agency demands payment by phone."

Simple, visible, memorable.

### Caller ID and Call Blocking

**UK:** Register with the Telephone Preference Service (TPS) at tpsonline.org.uk. This does not stop all scam calls but reduces nuisance marketing calls.

**UK:** Hiya and TrueCall are services that identify and block spam callers on mobile and landline.

**US:** Register at donotcall.gov. Use built-in iOS/Android spam call detection.

Set the phone to silence unknown callers:
- **iPhone**: Settings > Phone > Silence Unknown Callers: On
- **Android**: Phone app > Settings > Block numbers > Block unknown callers

Genuine contacts will leave a voicemail. Scammers rely on live conversation.

### Regular Check-In Calls

Establish a regular pattern of video or voice calls. Beyond the obvious family benefit, this creates a baseline — you will notice if something seems wrong, if they seem confused about a recent "investment opportunity" or "helpful technology call."

It also means they have less psychological need to respond to the social contact that many scams (especially romance scams) exploit.

## Phase 5: After an Incident — What to Do

If your elder has been targeted, time is critical.

### Immediate Steps (First 30 Minutes)

1. **Stop all contact** with the scammer — block the number/email/account
2. **Do not turn off the device yet** — a forensic scan may be needed if remote access occurred
3. **Change passwords** on email, banking, and social media accounts from a different, clean device
4. **Contact the bank** — report fraud, freeze cards, request a callback monitor on the account
5. **Run a full antivirus scan** on any device the scammer touched

### If Remote Access Was Granted

If the scammer had remote access to the device:
- Assume all passwords entered or visible on screen are compromised
- Assume any files visible on screen may have been copied
- Consider a factory reset of the device after backing up personal files
- Run Bitdefender or similar on the device before trusting any data from it

### Reporting

- **UK**: Action Fraud — actionfraud.police.uk — 0300 123 2040
- **US**: FTC — reportfraud.ftc.gov
- **Gift card fraud**: Contact the gift card issuer immediately — Walmart, Amazon, Google, Apple all have fraud reporting lines and can sometimes cancel unused card balances

### Emotional Support

Elder fraud has significant psychological consequences. Victims often feel shame, embarrassment, and distrust of technology and social contact. Avoid language that implies blame ("you should have known"). Focus on the fact that these scams fool security professionals — they are designed by criminal enterprises specifically to be convincing. Your elder's vulnerability was not a personal failing.

## Conclusion

Protecting elderly family members online requires a combination of technical setup, honest conversation, and ongoing support. The tech is the easy part — Bitdefender runs itself, automatic updates are a one-time setup, and browser protection is set-and-forget. The conversation is harder but more important. The scams that take the most money are not the ones that bypass antivirus software — they are the ones that bypass human judgment through emotional manipulation.

Give your elder the technical protection of a layered security setup, the cognitive tools to recognize manipulation, and the social safety net of a designated person to call. That combination stops the vast majority of attacks that are currently costing elderly people billions of dollars and pounds every year.
